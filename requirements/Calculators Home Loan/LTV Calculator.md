# Loan-to-Value (LTV) Calculator

## What is my LTV and what does it mean for my mortgage?

A loan-to-value calculator tells you exactly what percentage of a property's value you are borrowing, which LTV band you fall into, and how far your current deposit or equity sits from the next lending threshold — all in one instant calculation.

LTV is one of the most important numbers in any mortgage decision. Lenders use it to assess risk: the more of the property you own outright, the lower the risk to the lender, and lower risk almost always means better rates and wider product choice. A borrower at 60% LTV will typically access significantly better rates than a borrower at 90% LTV on the same property at the same time. Knowing your exact LTV — and understanding how close you are to the next band — helps you decide whether to save a larger deposit, time a remortgage, or compare products more effectively.

## What does an LTV calculator help me answer?

This calculator is built to answer the practical questions that arise before a purchase, remortgage, or product switch:

- What is my current loan-to-value?
- Which LTV band do I fall into?
- How much deposit do I need to reach the next LTV threshold?
- What risk level does my LTV represent to lenders?
- How much equity do I have relative to the loan?
- What happens to my LTV if I change the deposit or loan amount?

## What this LTV calculator shows

The calculator converts a property value and loan or deposit figure into an LTV percentage, assigns it to a risk band, and shows how far you are from each target threshold.

### Inputs

- **Property Value** — the purchase price or current valuation of the property
- **Input Mode** — choose whether to enter a loan amount directly or derive it from a deposit
- **Loan Amount** — the amount being borrowed; active when Loan Amount mode is selected
- **Deposit Type** — when Deposit mode is active, choose to enter the deposit as a cash amount or as a percentage of the property value
- **Deposit Amount** — the cash deposit; active when Deposit mode and Amount type are selected
- **Deposit Percent** — the deposit as a share of property value; active when Deposit mode and Percent type are selected

### Outputs

- **Current LTV** — the loan-to-value percentage, calculated from the inputs
- **LTV Band** — the standard lending band the LTV falls into, such as Up to 80% or Up to 90%
- **Risk Level** — the lending risk classification for the band: Lowest, Low, Moderate, Elevated, High, Very High, or Highest
- **Status** — whether the LTV is within a typical lending range or above common thresholds
- **Loan Share and Equity Share** — the proportion of the property value funded by the loan versus the deposit or equity
- **LTV Bands Table** — a reference table of all standard LTV bands with availability and risk descriptions
- **Deposit Targets Table** — shows exactly how much deposit you currently have, how much you need for each target LTV band, and how far you are from each one

## Why does LTV matter?

LTV is the single number most lenders look at when deciding what rate and products to offer. Even a small improvement in LTV — crossing from 85% to 80%, for example — can materially change the products available to a borrower.

The calculator is useful for:

- Checking whether saving a slightly larger deposit would move you into a better band before applying
- Understanding how much equity you have built up since you bought, relative to your remaining loan
- Planning a remortgage by seeing whether rising property values or loan repayments have lowered your LTV into a better risk category
- Testing the LTV impact of different purchase prices or deposit scenarios before making an offer
- Combining with a mortgage or remortgage calculator to model what a lower LTV could do to monthly payments and total interest

## How does an LTV calculator work?

The formula is straightforward. LTV is a ratio of what you owe to what the property is worth.

### LTV formula

$$\text{LTV} = \frac{\text{Loan Amount}}{\text{Property Value}} \times 100$$

Where:

- **Loan Amount** = the mortgage borrowing amount
- **Property Value** = the purchase price or current market valuation
- **LTV** = the result expressed as a percentage

### Deriving loan amount from deposit

When using Deposit mode, the loan amount is derived before the LTV is calculated:

$$\text{Loan Amount} = \text{Property Value} - \text{Deposit}$$

When deposit is entered as a percentage:

$$\text{Deposit} = \text{Property Value} \times \frac{\text{Deposit Percent}}{100}$$

### Deriving deposit from LTV target

The Deposit Targets table works backwards from each target LTV level to show how much deposit is required:

$$\text{Required Deposit} = \text{Property Value} \times \left(1 - \frac{\text{Target LTV}}{100}\right)$$

$$\text{Extra Deposit Needed} = \text{Required Deposit} - \text{Current Deposit}$$

If the extra deposit needed is zero or negative, you have already reached or passed that target band.

## What do the calculator inputs mean?

### Property Value

The market value of the property — either the agreed purchase price for a new buy, or the current valuation estimate for a remortgage or equity check. The accuracy of this figure is critical because all LTV outputs depend on it.

### Input Mode

Determines how the loan amount is calculated. Loan Amount mode lets you enter the borrowing directly. Deposit mode derives the loan by subtracting the deposit from the property value. Both produce the same LTV when run with matching numbers.

### Loan Amount

The size of the mortgage being considered. For a purchase, this is the total borrowing. For a remortgage check, it is the outstanding balance.

### Deposit Type

When using Deposit mode, you can enter the deposit as a cash amount or as a percentage of the property value. Percentage mode is useful for quick scenario testing across different property prices without recalculating.

### Deposit Amount

The cash amount available as a deposit. For a remortgage, this is equivalent to your current equity — the difference between the outstanding loan balance and the property value.

### Deposit Percent

The deposit as a share of the property value. Entering a percentage instead of a fixed amount makes it faster to test different LTV scenarios at various price points.

## What do the calculator outputs mean?

### Current LTV

The core output. This percentage tells you exactly how much of the property is financed by borrowing. 80% LTV means 80% loan and 20% equity or deposit.

### LTV Band

The standard lending band the LTV falls into. Lenders typically group products and pricing by LTV bands — 60%, 75%, 80%, 85%, 90%, 95%. Knowing your band tells you which tier of product you are likely to access.

### Risk Level

The risk classification that typically applies at each band:

| LTV Band | Risk Level |
| --- | --- |
| 60% or lower | Lowest risk |
| Up to 75% | Low risk |
| Up to 80% | Moderate risk |
| Up to 85% | Elevated risk |
| Up to 90% | High risk |
| Up to 95% | Very high risk |
| Above 95% | Highest risk |

Lower risk bands typically offer better rates, wider product choice, and easier approval. Higher risk bands usually mean fewer lenders, higher rates, and more stringent checks.

### Loan Share and Equity Share

These two numbers show how the property value is split between borrowing and ownership. Together they always total 100% of the property value. A higher equity share means a lower LTV and typically a stronger mortgage position.

### LTV Bands Table

A reference showing all standard lending bands, their typical product availability, and risk classification. Your current LTV band is highlighted so you can see at a glance where you sit in the market.

### Deposit Targets Table

Shows each target LTV threshold, how much deposit would be needed to reach it, and how much additional deposit is required beyond what you currently have. This is the most actionable output for borrowers planning a deposit savings strategy before a purchase or remortgage.

## How to use the LTV calculator

1. **Enter the property value.** Use the purchase price for a new buy, or your current property valuation for an equity or remortgage check.
2. **Choose your input mode.** Select Loan Amount if you know the borrowing figure. Select Deposit if you want to enter your deposit and let the calculator work out the loan.
3. **Enter the loan or deposit.** Use the slider to dial in the amount, or enter a deposit percentage if testing different scenarios.
4. **Read the LTV and band.** The result panel shows your current LTV percentage, which lending band it falls into, and the risk level.
5. **Check the Funding Split section.** This shows the loan and equity share proportions visually.
6. **Review the LTV Bands Table.** See where your result sits across all standard lending thresholds.
7. **Check the Deposit Targets Table.** See how much more deposit is needed to reach each key band — 60%, 75%, 80%, 85%, 90%.
8. **Adjust inputs to test scenarios.** Try a larger deposit to see how close you are to the next band. Try a lower property price to see the LTV impact.

## Example: what is the LTV on a 280,000 loan against a 350,000 property?

Using the calculator defaults:

| Input | Example Value |
| --- | --- |
| Property Value | 350,000 |
| Loan Amount | 280,000 |
| Input Mode | Loan Amount |

| Output | Result |
| --- | --- |
| Current LTV | 80% |
| Deposit | 70,000 (20%) |
| LTV Band | Up to 80% |
| Risk Level | Moderate |
| Status | Within Typical Range |

$$\text{LTV} = \frac{280{,}000}{350{,}000} \times 100 = 80\%$$

In this example, the borrower is exactly at the 80% threshold. To reach the 75% band, they would need a deposit of 87,500 — an additional 17,500 beyond the current 70,000. The Deposit Targets table would show this gap immediately.

Now change the loan to 262,500. The LTV drops to 75%:

$$\text{LTV} = \frac{262{,}500}{350{,}000} \times 100 = 75\%$$

That moves the borrower into the Low risk band, which could open access to better product pricing. The difference between Moderate and Low risk is 17,500 in this example — a meaningful but potentially achievable deposit increase for many buyers.

## How close am I to the next LTV band?

The Deposit Targets table answers this directly. For each standard threshold — 60%, 75%, 80%, 85%, 90%, 95% — it shows:

- The deposit currently in place
- The deposit required to hit that target
- The gap between the two

| Target LTV | Required Deposit (on 350,000 property) | Deposit at 280,000 loan | Extra Needed |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 95% | 17,500 | 70,000 | Already reached |
| 90% | 35,000 | 70,000 | Already reached |
| 85% | 52,500 | 70,000 | Already reached |
| 80% | 70,000 | 70,000 | At threshold |
| 75% | 87,500 | 70,000 | 17,500 more |
| 60% | 140,000 | 70,000 | 70,000 more |

This table makes the decision very tangible: a borrower at 80% is 17,500 away from the Low risk band, and 70,000 away from the Lowest risk band. Whether that gap is worth closing — through saving, negotiating a lower price, or waiting — depends on the rate improvement available at the next band.

## Important Notes

- This calculator uses the standard LTV formula: loan divided by property value times 100. It does not factor in lender-specific valuation adjustments or surveyor valuations that may differ from the purchase price.
- LTV bands shown are indicative reference ranges. Individual lenders may apply slightly different thresholds, and product availability within each band will vary by lender, product type, and market conditions.
- Risk classifications are general descriptions of typical lending behaviour at each band. They are not formal credit risk assessments and do not represent any lender's specific underwriting criteria.
- The Deposit Targets table shows the deposit required to reach each LTV threshold based purely on the current property value. If property values change, both the LTV and the required deposits change accordingly.
- LTV is one important factor in mortgage affordability and eligibility, but lenders also assess income, existing debts, credit history, and monthly affordability. A favourable LTV does not guarantee any specific rate or product approval.
- Results are estimates only. Users should validate LTV figures with their mortgage lender, broker, or conveyancer before making a purchase or remortgage decision.

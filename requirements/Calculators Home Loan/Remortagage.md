# Remortgage Calculator

## How much could I save by remortgaging?

A remortgage calculator estimates whether switching from your current mortgage rate and term to a new one could lower your monthly payments and improve your total borrowing cost over a chosen time horizon. It helps you compare the old and new repayment paths before making a refinancing decision.

Remortgaging is not only about chasing a lower interest rate. It is about understanding how your current balance, remaining term, new rate, and new term work together. A lower rate may reduce your payment, but a longer new term can also change the total amount paid over time. That is why a remortgage calculator is useful: it turns those moving parts into a clearer side-by-side comparison.

## What does a remortgage calculator tell me?

This calculator is built to answer several question-based searches users commonly have:

- How much could I save by remortgaging?
- Can I lower my monthly mortgage payment by switching rates?
- What is the break-even point on a remortgage?
- Why does changing the mortgage term affect my savings?
- Should I remortgage for lower monthly payments or shorter payoff time?

## What this remortgage calculator shows

The calculator is designed to answer a practical question: what happens if I switch from my current mortgage to a new one today?

### Inputs

- **Current Balance** — the amount you still owe on your mortgage
- **Current Rate (%)** — the interest rate on your existing mortgage
- **Remaining Term** — the time left on your existing mortgage
- **New Rate (%)** — the interest rate available on the remortgage deal
- **New Term** — how long the new remortgage would run
- **Horizon (Years)** — how far ahead you want to compare the two options

### Outputs

- **Monthly Saving** — the difference in monthly payments between your current and new mortgage
- **Annual Saving** — the monthly saving scaled to a 12-month estimate
- **Break-Even Month** — when the cumulative remortgage benefit turns positive
- **Total Saving** — the overall difference in cost across your selected horizon

## Why use a remortgage calculator before switching?

A remortgage can look attractive because the new interest rate is lower, but the real decision should be based on numbers rather than assumptions. A calculator helps you test affordability and savings before speaking to a lender or broker.

It is especially useful when you want to answer questions like:

- Can I lower my monthly mortgage payment?
- What is the difference between my current mortgage and a new rate?
- Why does a longer new term sometimes reduce the payment but not always improve long-term value?
- How much could I save over the next few years if I remortgage now?

## How does a remortgage calculator work?

At a high level, the calculator compares two repayment paths:

1. Stay on the current mortgage
2. Switch to the new mortgage terms

For each option, it estimates the monthly repayment using the loan balance, interest rate, and remaining or new term. It then compares those payments over your selected horizon.

### Monthly mortgage payment formula

For a standard repayment mortgage, the monthly payment is estimated with this formula:

$$M = P \times \frac{r \times (1 + r)^n}{(1 + r)^n - 1}$$

Where:

- **M** = monthly payment
- **P** = loan principal or current balance
- **r** = monthly interest rate
- **n** = total number of monthly payments

To convert an annual rate into a monthly rate:

$$r = \frac{\text{annual rate}}{12}$$

If rates are shown as percentages, divide by 100 first.

### Monthly saving formula

Once the calculator finds the old monthly payment and the new monthly payment, it estimates:

$$\text{Monthly Saving} = \text{Current Monthly Payment} - \text{New Monthly Payment}$$

If the result is positive, the new mortgage is cheaper per month. If it is negative, the new mortgage costs more each month.

**Example:**

- Current payment = 1,420
- New payment = 1,260
- Monthly saving = 1,420 − 1,260 = **160**

### Annual saving formula

$$\text{Annual Saving} = \text{Monthly Saving} \times 12$$

This gives a quick year-based estimate, which is easier for many borrowers to interpret than a monthly figure alone.

**Example:**

- Monthly saving = 160
- Annual saving = 160 × 12 = **1,920**

### Total saving over the selected horizon

The calculator compares the two scenarios over the number of years chosen in the Horizon field.

$$\text{Total Saving} = \text{Total Cost of Current Mortgage Path} - \text{Total Cost of New Mortgage Path}$$

Both costs are measured over the chosen horizon only.

**Example:**

- Monthly saving = 160
- Horizon = 5 years (60 months)
- Total saving = 160 × 60 = **9,600**

This is useful because a remortgage decision is often about the next few years, not just the next month.

## What do the calculator inputs mean?

### Current Balance

This is the amount you still owe today. The calculator uses this as the starting loan amount for both the current mortgage comparison and the remortgage scenario.

### Current Rate (%)

This is the interest rate on your existing mortgage. A higher current rate usually makes remortgaging more attractive, especially if the new rate is meaningfully lower.

### Remaining Term

This is the time left on your existing mortgage. A shorter remaining term usually means higher monthly payments because the balance is being repaid faster.

### New Rate (%)

This is the interest rate available on the remortgage deal you want to test. Lower rates often reduce the monthly payment, but they should always be considered together with the new term.

### New Term

This is how long the new remortgage would run. Extending the term can reduce monthly payments, but it may also increase how long you stay in debt.

### Horizon (Years)

This tells the calculator how far ahead to compare the two options. A 2-year, 3-year, or 5-year horizon is often useful when evaluating near-term savings.

## What do the remortgage calculator outputs mean?

### What is monthly saving?

Monthly Saving shows the payment difference between your current mortgage and the new mortgage. It answers the question: how much less will I pay each month if I remortgage?

This is often the first number users look at because monthly cash flow matters immediately.

### What is annual saving?

Annual Saving turns the monthly difference into a 12-month estimate. It makes the comparison easier to understand when planning a household budget.

### What is break-even month?

Break-Even shows when the remortgage starts to produce a positive cumulative benefit compared with staying on the current deal. In a simplified payment-only comparison, that can happen very early if the new monthly payment is lower.

In real-world decisions, borrowers often also consider lender fees, legal fees, valuation fees, or early repayment charges. Those can shift the true break-even point, even if the payment saving looks strong.

### What is total saving?

Total Saving measures the overall difference across your selected horizon. This is one of the most useful outputs because it stops you from focusing only on the monthly figure.

A lower monthly payment can look appealing, but total saving gives a broader view of whether the switch actually improves your position over the period you care about.

## How to use the remortgage calculator

Follow this process to get a more useful estimate:

1. **Enter your current mortgage balance.** Use the amount still outstanding, not the original loan size.
2. **Add your current interest rate.** Use the rate you are actually paying now.
3. **Enter the remaining term.** This should match the years or months left on your current mortgage.
4. **Enter the new remortgage rate.** Use the rate of the deal you want to compare.
5. **Choose the new term.** Try both matching and extended terms to see the trade-offs.
6. **Set a realistic horizon.** Pick the number of years you want the comparison to focus on.
7. **Review monthly, annual, and total savings together.**

## Example: can I save money by remortgaging?

Suppose you have:

| Input | Example Value |
| --- | --- |
| Current Balance | 220,000 |
| Current Rate | 6.2% |
| Remaining Term | 22 years |
| New Rate | 4.9% |
| New Term | 22 years |
| Horizon | 5 years |

In this kind of example, the new mortgage would usually produce a lower monthly payment because the interest rate drops while the term stays the same. That could lead to:

- lower monthly cost
- a noticeable annual saving
- positive cumulative savings within the chosen horizon

Now change only one variable: extend the new term from 22 years to 30 years. The monthly payment may fall even more, but that does not automatically mean the remortgage is better in every sense. You may improve short-term affordability while increasing the length of repayment.

That is why this calculator is useful for comparing multiple scenarios rather than testing only one.

## Why does new term matter so much?

Many people search for the lowest monthly payment, but the term is one of the biggest drivers of that result.

### Shorter new term

A shorter new term usually means:

- higher monthly payment
- faster principal repayment
- lower total interest over the long run

### Longer new term

A longer new term usually means:

- lower monthly payment
- slower balance reduction
- more flexibility in monthly budgeting
- potentially more interest over the life of the loan

This is why the best remortgage is not always the one with the lowest visible payment. It depends on whether your priority is cash flow today, faster payoff, or maximum savings over your chosen horizon.

| Comparison Point | Stay on Current Mortgage | Remortgage |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Monthly Payment | Based on current rate and remaining term | Based on new rate and new term |
| Annual Cost | May stay higher if rate is above market | May decrease if new terms are better |
| Flexibility | No change | Can reset term or payment structure |
| Horizon Saving | Baseline | Shows estimated gain or loss |

## What should users look at before trusting the result?

The calculator is a strong decision-support tool, but users should still interpret results carefully. The most important checks are:

- Is the new rate materially lower?
- Is the new term the same, shorter, or longer?
- Does the monthly saving still look good over the chosen horizon?
- Does the total saving support the switch, not just the first-month result?

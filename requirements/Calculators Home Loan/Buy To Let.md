# Buy-to-Let Mortgage Calculator

## What does a buy-to-let mortgage calculator show?

A buy-to-let mortgage calculator estimates whether a rental property could produce positive cash flow after mortgage costs, agent fees, vacancy, and running costs. It also helps compare gross yield, net yield, and rent cover so landlords can judge whether a property looks sustainable before buying.

A buy-to-let mortgage calculator is designed to answer a very practical question: will this property actually make money each month, and how strong is the deal overall? Instead of looking only at rent or only at the mortgage payment, the calculator brings the key parts together. It compares the property price, deposit, mortgage type, interest rate, rent, vacancy, and ongoing costs so users can see whether the investment still works after the main deductions are applied.

## What inputs does the calculator use?

The calculator works from the main property and finance details:

- **Property Price** — the purchase price or valuation figure
- **Monthly Rent** — expected rent collected each month
- **Deposit Type** — enter as an amount or as a percentage of the property price
- **Interest Rate (%)** — the mortgage interest rate
- **Loan Term (Years)** — the length of the mortgage
- **Mortgage Type** — Interest Only or Repayment

### Advanced inputs

The calculator also includes optional advanced inputs so the estimate is more realistic:

- **Rent Increase** — Off or On
- **Increase Type** — By Percentage or By Amount
- **Annual Increase** — the rate or amount applied each period
- **Increase Frequency (Years)** — how often the increase is applied
- **Vacancy Input** — entered as a percentage of rent lost, or as months per year
- **Letting Agent Fee (%)** — agent management fee as a share of rent
- **Maintenance / Monthly Costs** — ongoing repair and upkeep costs
- **Insurance / Service / Ground Rent** — fixed property charges

## What outputs does it show?

The calculator returns the key numbers landlords usually want first:

- **Estimated Net Monthly Cashflow** — what may be left each month after main costs
- **Monthly Mortgage** — the mortgage payment based on rate, term, and type
- **Annual Cashflow** — net monthly cashflow scaled to 12 months
- **Gross Yield** — annual rent as a percentage of property price
- **Net Yield** — net annual income as a percentage of property price
- **Stress Coverage** — how comfortably rent covers the mortgage payment

There is also a cashflow projection table, which is especially useful because rental property returns do not stay static forever. Rent may rise, vacancy may reduce collected income, and costs can make a property look very different over time than it does in a simple first-month estimate.

## How to use the buy-to-let mortgage calculator

The easiest way to use the calculator is to build the result in layers.

1. **Enter the property price.** This is the purchase price or valuation figure used to estimate the loan and yield.
2. **Add the monthly rent.** Use realistic expected rent, not a best-case number.
3. **Choose deposit type.** You can enter the deposit as an amount or as a percentage of the property price.
4. **Select the mortgage rate and term.** These determine the mortgage cost.
5. **Choose interest only or repayment.** This has a major effect on monthly cash flow.
6. **Add agent fees, vacancy, and running costs.** This is where many rental property calculations become more realistic.
7. **Turn on rent increases if needed.** This helps model longer-term projections rather than a flat-rent assumption.

The most important habit is to avoid entering only optimistic numbers. A buy-to-let property can look excellent on gross rent alone, but once vacancy, agent fees, maintenance, and mortgage costs are included, the result may change significantly.

## Why mortgage type matters so much

One of the most useful comparisons on a buy-to-let page is interest only vs repayment.

### Quick comparison table

| Mortgage Type | Usually Lower Monthly Payment | Builds Equity Faster | Usually Better Short-Term Cash Flow |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Interest Only | Yes | No | Yes |
| Repayment | No | Yes | Usually No |

### Interest-only buy-to-let mortgage

With an interest-only mortgage, the monthly payment is usually lower because you are mainly paying the interest cost, not clearing the loan balance through the regular payment. That often improves monthly cash flow.

This is why many landlords search:

- Is interest only better for buy-to-let
- Why is interest-only cash flow higher
- Can rent cover an interest-only mortgage more easily

### Repayment buy-to-let mortgage

With a repayment mortgage, the monthly payment is usually higher because it includes both interest and principal repayment. That can reduce monthly profit, but it also reduces the outstanding balance over time.

This is useful for users comparing:

- Interest only vs repayment buy-to-let
- Why does repayment reduce rental profit
- Should I choose lower cash flow or faster debt reduction

## What is estimated net monthly cash flow?

This is one of the most important outputs on the page.

Estimated Net Monthly Cashflow shows what may be left each month after the main property costs are deducted from effective rent. In simple terms, it answers:

> After rent comes in and the main costs go out, what is left?

That usually includes:

- mortgage payment
- vacancy impact
- letting agent fee
- maintenance costs
- insurance, service charge, or ground rent

This is the number most closely tied to real monthly affordability for a landlord.

## What is the difference between gross yield and net yield?

This is a very common question, and it matters because gross yield can make a property look better than it really is.

### Gross yield

Gross yield is a top-level return measure based on rent and property price only. It does not reflect agent fees, maintenance, insurance, or vacancy.

$$\text{Gross Yield} = \frac{\text{Annual Rent}}{\text{Property Price}} \times 100$$

It is useful for quick comparisons across properties.

### Net yield

Net yield goes one step further and includes operating costs. That makes it a better measure of real property performance.

$$\text{Net Yield} = \frac{\text{Net Annual Income}}{\text{Property Price}} \times 100$$

A property can have a strong gross yield but a weaker net yield if running costs are high. That is why many landlords search:

- What is a good net yield for buy-to-let
- Why is net yield lower than gross yield
- How to calculate rental yield after expenses

Net yield on this page is treated as income after operating costs but before mortgage finance costs, because mortgage impact is already shown separately in cash flow outputs.

## What is stress coverage?

Stress Coverage helps show how comfortably the rent covers the mortgage payment. It is a quick rent-to-mortgage strength check.

$$\text{Stress Coverage} = \frac{\text{Effective Monthly Rent}}{\text{Monthly Mortgage}} \times 100$$

A higher coverage figure usually means more breathing room. A lower figure means the property is more sensitive to vacancy, rate rises, or cost increases.

This is especially useful for search intent like:

- Can rent cover buy-to-let mortgage
- What is rental stress coverage
- Why does mortgage cover matter for landlords

Because lenders can apply different stress rules, this calculator presents Stress Coverage as a simple coverage view, not as a lender approval guarantee.

## Why vacancy and running costs matter

Many first-pass investment checks ignore vacancy and maintenance, which can make the deal look stronger than it is.

### Vacancy

A property may not be rented every single month of the year. Vacancy can be entered either as:

- a percentage of rent lost, or
- a number of vacant months per year

This helps answer:

- How does vacancy affect rental cash flow
- Why does rental profit drop even with good monthly rent

### Letting agent fee

If a managing agent takes a percentage of rent, that reduces the income available to cover mortgage and other costs.

### Maintenance and property costs

Maintenance, insurance, service charges, and ground rent can have a major effect on net cash flow. These are exactly the kinds of costs that separate a rough estimate from a more realistic one.

## What does the cashflow projection table do?

The cashflow projection table shows how the property may perform over time rather than only in the first month or first year.

That is important because rental property returns change when:

- rent increases over time
- vacancy assumptions reduce collected income each year
- operating costs continue across the term
- mortgage payments continue over the selected term

If rent increase is turned on, the table can project future rent using either:

- a percentage increase, or
- a fixed amount increase

## Buy-to-let mortgage formula reference

| Output | Formula |
| --- | --- |
| Loan Amount | Property Price − Deposit |
| Monthly Mortgage | Based on loan amount, rate, term, and mortgage type |
| Estimated Net Monthly Cashflow | Effective Rent − Mortgage − Agent Fee − Running Costs |
| Annual Cashflow | Net Monthly Cashflow × 12 |
| Gross Yield | Annual Rent ÷ Property Price × 100 |
| Net Yield | Net Annual Income ÷ Property Price × 100 |
| Stress Coverage | Effective Monthly Rent ÷ Monthly Mortgage × 100 |
| Projection Table | Update rent, apply vacancy and costs, then calculate future cashflow |

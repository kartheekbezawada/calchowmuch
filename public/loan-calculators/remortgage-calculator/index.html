Remortgage Guide

How much could I save by remortgaging?

A remortgage calculator estimates whether switching from your current mortgage rate and term to a new one could lower monthly payments and improve total borrowing cost over your chosen horizon.

Remortgaging is not only about finding a lower interest rate. Current balance, remaining term, new rate, and new term all interact, so this guide is designed to support a side-by-side decision.

What does a remortgage calculator tell me?

  • How much you could save by remortgaging.
  • Whether switching rates can lower your monthly payment.
  • When break-even can occur on a remortgage path.
  • How term changes can affect total savings.
  • How to compare lower payments versus shorter payoff.

What this remortgage calculator shows

The calculator compares staying on your current mortgage against switching to a new deal today.

Inputs

  • Current Balance: amount still owed.
  • Current Rate (%): rate on existing mortgage.
  • Remaining Term: time left on existing mortgage.
  • New Rate (%): remortgage rate to test.
  • New Term: duration of the remortgage term.
  • Horizon (Years): comparison horizon.

Outputs

  • Monthly Saving: current payment minus new payment.
  • Annual Saving: monthly saving multiplied by 12.
  • Break-Even Month: first month cumulative benefit becomes positive.
  • Total Saving: overall cost difference across the selected horizon.

Why use a remortgage calculator before switching?

A lower rate can look attractive, but rate alone does not decide value. Testing affordability and total savings before lender discussions helps avoid one-variable decisions.

How does a remortgage calculator work?

It estimates monthly repayment for two repayment paths, then compares those results over your selected horizon:

  1. Stay on current mortgage terms.
  2. Switch to new mortgage terms.

Monthly mortgage payment formula

M = P * (r * (1 + r)^n) / ((1 + r)^n - 1)
  • M: monthly payment
  • P: principal or current balance
  • r: monthly interest rate
  • n: number of monthly payments
r = annual_rate / 12

If your annual rate is a percentage, divide by 100 before applying the formula.

Saving formulas

Monthly Saving = Current Monthly Payment - New Monthly Payment
Annual Saving = Monthly Saving * 12
Total Saving = Total Current Path Cost - Total New Path Cost

Why term length matters

Term choice has major impact on affordability and long-run cost profile.

Shorter new term

  • Higher monthly payment.
  • Faster principal repayment.
  • Usually lower total long-run interest.

Longer new term

  • Lower monthly payment.
  • Slower balance reduction.
  • More payment flexibility.
  • Potentially higher total interest over full life.

What to check before trusting the result

  • Is the new rate materially lower than your current rate?
  • Is the new term shorter, equal, or longer?
  • Do savings still hold across the full horizon?
  • Does total saving support the switch, not just first-month payment?

What does this remortgage comparison show?

Why is horizon length important?

A longer horizon shows how differences compound over time, which can change your switching decision.

03

Can a lower rate still be a weak switch?

Yes. If the term changes materially, short-term monthly relief may not always mean strongest long-term value. Compare the baseline repayment schedule first in the Home Loan Calculator.

04

How should I read break-even month?

It is the first month where cumulative new-deal cost becomes lower than the current-deal cost.

05

Should I compare monthly and yearly table views?

Yes. Monthly reveals timing details, while yearly helps you evaluate strategy with less noise.

06

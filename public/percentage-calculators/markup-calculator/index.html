What Selling Price Does Your Markup Create?

The short answer: markup adds a percentage on top of cost so you can turn cost into a selling price.

This calculator lets you work in either direction: find the selling price from cost and markup, or work backward from cost and price to see the markup percentage.

Explanation

Markup is the amount added to cost to get selling price. Markup percentage is calculated relative to the cost.

For a single product, markup % can be used to calculate price from cost, or computed from cost and price.

For multiple products, basket markup uses totals so the result is weighted. Basket markup % is calculated by dividing the total markup amount (total price minus total cost) by the total cost and multiplying by 100.

If cost is zero for an item, markup % for that row cannot be calculated because division by zero is not possible.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is markup?

A: Markup is the amount added to cost to get the selling price.

Q: How do you calculate selling price from cost and markup %?

A: Multiply the cost by (1 + markup/100).

Q: How do you calculate markup % from cost and price?

A: Divide (price minus cost) by cost and multiply by 100.

Q: What is basket markup?

A: Basket markup is the markup calculated across multiple products using total cost and total price.

Q: How do you calculate weighted average markup?

A: Compute (total price minus total cost) divided by total cost, then multiply by 100.

Q: Is basket markup the same as averaging the item markups?

A: No, basket markup is weighted by costs (and quantities), so it can differ from a simple average.

Q: Can markup be negative?

A: Yes, if the selling price is lower than the cost, the markup is negative.

Q: What happens if cost is zero?

A: Markup percent is undefined because you cannot divide by zero.

Q: Is markup the same as profit margin?

A: No, markup is based on cost, while margin is based on selling price.

Q: Where is markup used in real life?

A: Markup is used in retail pricing, quoting, invoicing, and setting prices for product ranges.

Important Notes

  • Markup is based on cost: this is why markup and margin are not the same percentage.
  • Higher markup means higher selling price: if cost stays fixed, increasing markup raises price directly.
  • Basket markup is weighted: the combined result depends on total cost and total price, not a simple average of item markups.
  • Zero cost breaks markup percentage: markup percent cannot be calculated when the cost base is zero.

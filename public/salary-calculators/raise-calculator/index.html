How to Guide

What This Calculator Does

The calculator compares your current salary with the proposed raise and shows the new salary, the dollar increase, and the resulting percentage change. It works with either a percentage raise or a flat dollar amount.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Enter your current annual salary.
  2. Choose percent mode (e.g., 5%) or amount mode (e.g., $3,000).
  3. Enter the raise value.
  4. Click Calculate to see the raise amount, new salary, and percentage change.

Key Insight

A 3% raise on a typical salary barely keeps pace with inflation. If prices rose 3% and your raise was also 3%, your purchasing power stayed flat — you did not actually get ahead. Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate a raise?

Multiply your current salary by the raise percentage (e.g., $65,000 × 5% = $3,250 raise). Or enter a flat dollar amount directly.

Is a 3% raise good?

It depends on inflation. If inflation is also around 3%, a 3% raise keeps your purchasing power flat. You need to beat inflation to get ahead.

How do I compare a raise with a new job offer?

Enter your current salary and the raise to get the new salary, then compare it with the other offer. Do not forget to factor in bonuses, benefits, and commute costs.

What is a typical annual raise?

Annual merit raises in the U.S. typically range from 3–5%. Promotions and job changes often produce 10–20% increases.

Should I save 100% of my raise?

Not necessarily, but saving at least half of a raise before adjusting your lifestyle is a common strategy to build wealth without feeling restricted.

Can I model both percentage and flat raises?

Yes. Toggle between percent mode and amount mode to see how each approach affects your new salary.

Does this include taxes?

No. All figures are gross pay. Your take-home increase will be smaller after taxes and deductions.

Is my data saved or sent anywhere?

No. All calculations run locally in your browser. Nothing is stored or transmitted.

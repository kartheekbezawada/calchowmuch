How to Guide

What This Calculator Does

The calculator multiplies your hourly rate by hours worked to produce gross weekly pay. In split mode, it separates regular and overtime hours so you can see the overtime premium. It also projects annualized pay when you enter weeks per year.

When you need to turn that weekly result into a monthly planning number, use the Annual to Monthly Salary Calculator or the broader Salary Calculator. The full Salary Calculators hub is the quickest way to compare the rest of the pay-conversion tools in this cluster.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Enter your hourly rate.
  2. Choose standard mode (total hours) or split mode (regular + overtime hours).
  3. Enter your weekly hours. In split mode, enter regular hours and overtime hours separately, plus the overtime multiplier.
  4. Enter weeks per year to see the annualized projection (use 50 for two unpaid vacation weeks, or 52 for full-year).
  5. Click Calculate to see weekly and annualized gross pay.

Key Insight

Working 50 weeks vs. 52 weeks at $25/hr drops annual income by $2,000 — that is two unpaid vacation weeks. Before annualizing, decide whether you are counting paid weeks only or calendar weeks. The difference matters for accurate budgeting.

Real-World Example

Marcus works as a warehouse supervisor earning $22/hr. His standard week is 40 hours ($880), but during holiday season (October–December) he picks up 10 hours of overtime at 1.5×. His OT rate is $33/hr, adding $330 to his weekly pay — a total of $1,210/week during peak season.

Over 12 peak weeks, that extra overtime earns him $3,960 — enough to cover holiday expenses without touching savings. Outside peak season, his standard $880/week × 38 remaining weeks gives him $33,440 — for a full-year total of about $48,160.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate weekly pay from my hourly rate?

Multiply your hourly rate by total hours worked that week. For $22/hr and 40 hours, weekly pay is $880.

What is split mode and when should I use it?

Split mode separates regular and overtime hours. Use it when you work over 40 hours and your overtime pays at a higher rate (like 1.5×).

Should I annualize with 50 or 52 weeks?

Use 52 if you are paid every week of the year (including PTO). Use 50 if you have two unpaid vacation weeks. The difference is two full weeks of pay.

Why does my annualized pay seem high?

Annualized pay assumes you repeat the same weekly hours for every counted week. If your hours vary, annualized is an estimate — not a guarantee.

Can I compare two jobs with different weekly hours?

Yes. Run each scenario separately and compare the weekly and annualized figures side by side.

Does this include taxes or deductions?

No. This calculates gross weekly pay only. Your take-home depends on tax bracket, insurance, and retirement contributions.

Is weekly pay better for budgeting than monthly?

For shift workers and variable-hour employees, yes. Weekly pay matches the rhythm of hours worked. Salaried workers may prefer monthly.

Is my data saved or sent anywhere?

No. All calculations run locally in your browser. Nothing is stored or transmitted.

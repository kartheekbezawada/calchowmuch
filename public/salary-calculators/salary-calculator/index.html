Salary planning guide

Turn one pay number into a complete picture of your earnings

You have a salary figure from an offer letter, a paycheck, or a job listing — but you need to know what that number actually means per month, per week, or per hour. This calculator converts one gross-pay amount into every common pay period so you can budget, compare offers, or negotiate with real numbers instead of guesswork.

How to Guide

What This Calculator Does

You enter a single pay amount — annual, monthly, hourly, or any other period — and the calculator maps it across all major pay periods: annual, monthly, biweekly, weekly, daily, and hourly. Every result updates when you adjust your schedule assumptions.

This lets you answer questions like "What does $65,000 a year look like per month?" or "Which offer pays more per hour once I factor in the expected schedule?"

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Find the pay figure you already know — from an offer letter, paycheck stub, or job listing.
  2. Select the pay frequency that matches it (annual, monthly, hourly, etc.).
  3. Confirm your schedule: hours per week, weeks per year, and workdays per week. The default is 40/52/5 — adjust if the role differs.
  4. Click Calculate and compare the results to your budget, another offer, or your current pay.

Annual salary stays $60,000 in every row. Only the per-hour and per-week breakdowns shift.

The key takeaway: a higher annual salary does not always mean better pay. A $60,000 role with 45-hour weeks pays $3.21 less per hour than the same salary with 40-hour weeks. Over a year, that hidden gap adds up to thousands of dollars in uncompensated time.

Key Insight

The same $60,000 salary pays $28.85 per hour with 40-hour weeks but only $25.64 per hour with 45-hour weeks. Those 5 extra unpaid hours each week quietly cost you $3.21 on every hour worked — the equivalent of giving back over $6,600 a year in uncompensated time.

Real-World Example

Jamie is weighing two offers. A marketing agency pays $72,000 per year but expects 45-hour weeks. A government role pays $65,000 per year with a strict 37.5-hour week. At first glance, $72,000 beats $65,000 by $7,000.

But running both through the calculator with their actual schedules tells a different story:

  • Agency: $72,000 ÷ (45 hrs × 52 wks) = $30.77 per hour
  • Government: $65,000 ÷ (37.5 hrs × 52 wks) = $33.33 per hour

The lower-paying offer actually values Jamie's time $2.56 more per hour. That is why comparing the hourly rate — not just the headline salary — matters when schedules differ.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I compare two job offers with this calculator?

Run each offer separately using its own schedule (hours per week and weeks per year). Compare the hourly rate — it reveals the true value after schedule differences are removed.

What is gross pay and why does it matter here?

Gross pay is your earnings before taxes, retirement contributions, and insurance are deducted. Comparing gross pay lets you evaluate roles without individual tax situations clouding the picture.

Why does my hourly rate drop when I add more hours?

The same annual salary is spread across more working hours. A 45-hour week produces a lower hourly rate than a 40-hour week on the same salary — even though the annual total stays the same.

Does the number of paid weeks really matter?

Yes. Working 50 instead of 52 weeks raises your weekly and hourly rates, but your annual total stays the same. Unpaid time off changes the per-period breakdown more than most people expect.

Why are monthly pay and biweekly pay different amounts?

Monthly divides annual pay by 12 (calendar months). Biweekly divides by 26 (every two weeks for a year). They split the same total differently, which matters when you budget around paycheck timing.

Can I use this for part-time or freelance schedules?

Yes. Set hours per week and weeks per year to match your actual schedule. The calculator adjusts every pay period accordingly — no need to do manual scaling.

Should I compare salaries by annual amount or hourly rate?

Use annual for big-picture budgeting. Use hourly when schedules differ between offers — it normalizes for time worked and exposes hidden gaps in how your time is valued.

What schedule assumptions does the calculator default to?

40 hours per week, 52 weeks per year, and 5 workdays per week. Change any of these to match your actual or expected schedule for an accurate conversion.

Does this account for taxes or take-home pay?

No. This converts gross pay across time periods. Your take-home depends on tax bracket, state, deductions, and benefits — variables that change by person and paycheck.

Is my data saved or sent anywhere?

No. Everything runs locally in your browser. Nothing is stored or transmitted to any server.

What This Result Means

Investment return becomes more useful when you stop treating the final balance as the only answer. A stronger portfolio result is not necessarily a better result if it requires outsized contributions, unrealistic return assumptions, or hides most of its apparent strength behind inflation.

That is why this page surfaces both nominal and real views. A nominal CAGR can look attractive while the real CAGR remains modest. A healthy ending balance can still be less impressive once tax drag and contribution burden are brought into the picture.

  • Contributions show how much new capital you supplied.
  • Profit shows what the portfolio generated above contributions.
  • Real metrics show what the result may still be worth after inflation.

What Usually Changes The Interpretation Most

Duration is often the most underestimated lever because it gives both contributions and returns more time to work. Contribution timing matters too. Beginning-of-period deposits usually finish slightly ahead of end-of-period deposits because they start compounding earlier.

The Core Return Metrics Behind The Model

This calculator is more advanced than a simple future-value tool because it layers in portfolio performance measures, inflation adjustment, and tax drag. The two most useful summary metrics are nominal CAGR and real CAGR.

CAGR = (Ending Value ÷ Starting Capital)1 / n - 1

Real CAGR ≈ (1 + Nominal CAGR) ÷ (1 + Inflation) - 1

  • Nominal CAGR shows the annualized growth rate before inflation.
  • Real CAGR shows the annualized growth rate after inflation drag.
  • Tax drag reduces how much of the nominal gain you actually keep.

How To Use The Advanced Inputs Well

Advanced settings are best used for comparison, not decoration. Variable annual returns, tax assumptions, contribution timing, and one-off events become valuable when they help you compare a baseline case against a conservative case and a stronger case.

The weakest use of advanced mode is turning on every option at once without a clear decision question. The strongest use is stress-testing a believable plan and seeing which assumptions actually change the interpretation in a meaningful way.

When This Calculator Is The Right Tool

Use this route when you need more than an ending balance. If the question is simple accumulation, the investment growth calculator may be enough. If the focus is purely on compounding mechanics, the compound interest calculator is simpler. This route is for portfolio planning where tax, inflation, and return quality all matter.

Bottom Line

A portfolio can finish with a large balance and still tell a weak planning story. This calculator is valuable because it forces the supporting metrics into view: contributions, profit, tax, nominal CAGR, and real purchasing power.

Frequently Asked Questions

01

How is investment return calculated here?

The model combines compound growth, recurring contributions, and optional tax and inflation adjustments.

02

Can I model both lump sum and regular deposits?

Yes. You can enter an initial amount and recurring contributions with monthly, quarterly, or annual frequency.

03

What does CAGR mean?

CAGR is the annualized growth rate implied by your starting amount and projected ending value.

04

How does inflation affect results?

Inflation reduces purchasing power. Real output values show what the ending balance may be worth in today’s terms.

05

How is tax applied?

Tax is applied at year end to net positive gains after any modeled offsets.

06

Can I input year-by-year return assumptions?

Yes. Advanced mode lets you set a specific return percentage for each year in the plan.

07

Can I simulate a market crash?

Yes. You can apply a one-year percentage drop in a selected year to stress-test the projection.

08

Why do monthly and annual views differ?

They show the same model outputs at different granularity, so month-level tables reveal more path detail.

09

Are these projections guaranteed?

No. This tool is for education and planning. Real market returns can differ materially from assumptions.

10

What is the best way to use this tool?

Start with a realistic baseline, then compare conservative and optimistic variants one variable at a time.

Investment Growth & Future Value

Calculate the future value of your investments using compound interest and regular contributions. Use this tool to forecast wealth accumulation, retirement savings, or long-term financial goals.

Compound Interest Power: Your money grows faster over time because you earn interest on both your principal and previous interest.

Consistency Matters: Regular monthly contributions significantly boost your final balance, often more than a slightly higher return rate.

Inflation Reality: Always consider inflation. A million dollars in 20 years won't buy what it does today.

How It Works

  • Initial Investment: The starting amount you invest today.
  • Expected Return: The annual percentage growth rate (historically 7-10% for stocks).
  • Contributions: Money added regularly (e.g., monthly from your paycheck).
  • Compounding: Frequency at which earnings are reinvested (Daily to Annually).

The Future Value Formula

FV = P × (1 + r)ⁿ + PMT × [((1 + r)ⁿ - 1) / r]
  • FV = Future Value
  • P = Principal (Initial Investment)
  • PMT = Monthly Contribution
  • r = Periodic Interest Rate (Annual Rate / Periods per Year)
  • n = Total Number of Periods (Years × Periods per Year)

Frequently Asked Questions

What is investment growth?
Investment growth refers to the increase in value of an asset or portfolio over time, driven by compound interest, capital appreciation, and reinvested dividends.
How does inflation affect my investment?
Inflation erodes purchasing power. If your investment grows by 7% but inflation is 3%, your "real" return is approximately 4%.
What is a realistic rate of return?
The S&P 500 has historically returned about 10% annually related interest. Adjusted for inflation, this is often cited as 7%. Bonds typically return less (3-5%).
Why is compounding frequency important?
More frequent compounding (e.g., monthly vs. annually) means your interest earns interest sooner, resulting in a slightly higher future value.
Should I contribute monthly or annually?
Monthly contributions are generally better because your money starts working for you sooner, rather than sitting in a checking account waiting for an annual deposit.
Does this calculate taxes?
No. This calculator shows pre-tax growth. Investment gains may be subject to capital gains tax depending on the account type (e.g., brokerage vs. Roth IRA).
What is the Rule of 72?
It's a quick way to estimate doubling time. Divide 72 by your interest rate. At 7% return, money doubles in roughly 10.2 years (72 ÷ 7).
What happens if the market drops?
This calculator assumes a constant average return. Real markets fluctuate (volatility). Over long periods (10+ years), volatility tends to smooth out towards the average.
Can I use this for retirement planning?
Yes! It's perfect for estimating how much your 401(k) or IRA might be worth at retirement based on your current savings rate.
What is "Time to Double"?
The time it takes for your investment to double in value. At 10%, it's about 7.2 years. At 5%, it's about 14.4 years.

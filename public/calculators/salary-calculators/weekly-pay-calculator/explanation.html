Weekly earnings guide

How a weekly pay calculator turns hours worked into gross weekly income

Use this gross weekly pay calculator to estimate what you will earn in a typical week. Enter hourly rate and hours, or split regular and overtime hours, then calculate to see weekly pay first and the annualized equivalent second.

Weekly pay is often the clearest answer for shift work, freelancing, overtime planning, and short-term budgeting because it keeps the cash-flow question simple before you annualize it.

How to Guide

What This Calculator Does

The calculator estimates gross weekly pay from hourly wages and hours worked. It can also project annualized pay when you enter weeks per year.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Enter your hourly rate.
  2. Choose standard mode or split mode.
  3. Add total weekly hours or separate regular and overtime hours.
  4. Enter weeks per year if you want an annualized estimate.
  5. Click Calculate to review the weekly and annualized figures.

How to Interpret Results

Result What it tells you
Higher weekly pay Your rate, hours, or overtime multiplier is higher.
Higher annualized pay You are assuming more paid weeks across the year.
No annualized result You have not entered weeks per year yet.

Weekly pay is best for short-term budgeting and shift planning. The annualized figure is only a projection, so it is most useful when your work pattern stays fairly steady.

Formula breakdown: standard weekly pay equals hourly rate times total weekly hours. Split mode adds regular pay and overtime pay, then annualized pay equals weekly pay times weeks per year.

Real-World Example

If you earn $22.00 per hour and work 38 hours in a week, your weekly pay is $836.00. If you keep that pace for 50 weeks, the annualized estimate is $41,800.00.

Weekly Pay vs Annualized Income

Weekly pay shows what a normal workweek is worth right now. Annualized pay turns that weekly pace into a rough yearly estimate, which is useful for planning but not a guarantee.

If your hours vary a lot, the weekly result is usually more reliable than the annualized one. Use the yearly figure as a planning range, not a promise.

Grow Your Wealth

A weekly number is useful for building a repeatable savings habit. Setting aside part of one week’s pay on each payday can be easier than waiting for a large monthly transfer.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do you calculate weekly pay?

Multiply the hourly rate by total weekly hours, or split regular and overtime hours if that mode is enabled.

Does this show gross or take-home pay?

It shows gross weekly pay only before taxes, deductions, retirement contributions, or benefits.

Can this include overtime hours?

Yes. Split mode can estimate weekly pay using separate regular and overtime inputs plus an overtime multiplier.

Can I annualize the weekly result?

Yes. The weekly result can be multiplied by weeks per year to estimate annualized pay.

Does this work for part-time schedules?

Yes. The calculator works for any schedule as long as you enter the hours that match your workweek.

Does the result include tax deductions?

No. It estimates gross weekly pay only.

What does annualized pay mean here?

It is a simple projection that multiplies one typical week by the number of paid weeks you enter.

Why keep split mode optional?

Most users want the fast weekly answer first, so total-hours mode stays simple and overtime appears only when needed.

Should I budget from weekly pay or annualized pay?

Use weekly pay for near-term cash flow and annualized pay for rough yearly planning when your schedule stays stable.

Can I compare overtime scenarios with this?

Yes. Change regular hours, overtime hours, or the multiplier to see how weekly earnings move.

Are my inputs stored?

No. All calculations run locally in your browser - no data is stored.

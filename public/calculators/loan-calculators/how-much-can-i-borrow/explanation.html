How To Guide

How much can I borrow?

How much you can borrow depends on your income, existing debts, interest rate, loan term, deposit, and the lender's affordability rules. In most cases, borrowing power rises when income is stable, debts are lower, and the repayment period is longer, but total interest usually increases as well.

What does a how much can I borrow calculator tell me?

This calculator is built to answer some of the most common questions people have when they start thinking about borrowing for a home:

  • How much could I borrow based on my income and debts?
  • Can I increase my borrowing amount by lowering debts or expenses?
  • How does changing the interest rate affect borrowing power?
  • Why does changing the loan term change how much I can borrow?
  • Should I borrow the maximum amount or choose a safer borrowing limit?

What affects how much I can borrow?

A borrowing calculator estimates how large a loan may be affordable based on your finances. It is not just about salary. Lenders usually want to know whether the payment fits comfortably into your monthly budget after other obligations are covered.

  • Income: higher and more stable income usually supports a larger loan.
  • Existing debt: car loans, credit cards, student loans, and personal loans reduce borrowing power.
  • Interest rate: higher rates reduce how much principal the same monthly payment can support.
  • Loan term: a longer term lowers monthly payments and may increase borrowing capacity.
  • Deposit or down payment: a larger upfront contribution can reduce risk and improve terms.
  • Credit profile: stronger credit may help you qualify for better rates.
  • Living costs and commitments: regular expenses matter because affordability is about real cash flow.

How do lenders calculate how much I can borrow?

Most borrowing decisions combine two ideas: income-based affordability and payment-based affordability.

Gross Monthly Income = Annual Income / 12

Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI) = (Total Monthly Debt Payments / Gross Monthly Income) x 100

A lower DTI usually means more room for a new loan payment. If you earn 5,000 per month before tax and already pay 800 toward debts, your DTI is 16%.

Loan Amount = M x [1 - (1 + r)^-n] / r

  • M: monthly payment you can afford.
  • r: monthly interest rate.
  • n: total number of monthly payments.

If the rate rises, the same payment supports a smaller loan. If the term gets longer, the same payment supports a bigger loan, but you pay interest for more years.

Loan-to-Value (LTV) = (Loan Amount / Property Value) x 100

Example: if the property costs 300,000 and the loan is 240,000, the LTV is 80%. Lower LTV often reduces risk for the lender and may improve your available options.

Can I increase how much I can borrow?

  • Lower existing debt: improves DTI and frees monthly cash flow.
  • Increase your deposit: reduces required loan size and may improve terms.
  • Improve your credit profile: may help you qualify for better rates.
  • Choose a longer term carefully: can raise borrowing power but usually increases total interest.
  • Apply with realistic numbers: accurate inputs produce better decisions.

Safe borrowing amount versus maximum borrowing amount

The maximum borrowing amount is the upper estimate a lender or calculator may show. The safe borrowing amount is what still leaves room in your budget for savings, emergencies, repairs, rising costs, and normal life.

A useful rule is to compare the estimated payment against your full monthly budget, not only lender approval logic.

  • Emergency savings
  • Transport costs
  • Utilities
  • Childcare or family support
  • Insurance
  • Maintenance and repairs
  • Future rate changes where applicable

Worked Example

Suppose gross monthly income is 6,000 and existing monthly debts are 700. After reviewing your budget, 1,800 per month is a comfortable maximum for the new loan. If the rate is 5.5% and the term is 25 years, the calculator estimates how much principal that payment can support. If the rate increases, the estimate drops. If the term increases, the estimate rises.

This is why a borrowing calculator is best used as a planning tool. It helps you understand the moving parts before you apply.

Frequently Asked Questions

01

Is this a guaranteed amount I can borrow?

No. This is only an estimate. Final approval depends on your credit score, employment stability, and the lender's own policies and affordability criteria.

02

Why is my borrowing less than 5x my income?

Because lenders adjust for your monthly expenses, existing debt commitments, and interest rate stress tests that simulate higher future rates.

03

Can I borrow more with a longer loan term?

Often yes. A longer term lowers monthly payments and can increase affordability, but you will pay significantly more interest over the life of the loan. After estimating borrowing power, test the actual repayment profile in the Home Loan Calculator.

04

Do different lenders give different amounts?

Yes. Each lender has its own criteria for income multiples, expense allowances, and stress test rates. Shopping around or using a broker can help.

05

What is an income multiple?

An income multiple is a simple multiplier applied to your annual salary. For example, at 4.5x on a 90,000 salary, the maximum loan would be 405,000.

06

How does the payment-to-income method work?

It calculates the maximum monthly payment you can afford after subtracting expenses and debts from your monthly income, then works backwards to find the loan amount.

07

Does a larger deposit increase how much I can borrow?

A deposit increases your total purchasing power but does not directly change the loan amount. Lenders base borrowing on income and affordability, not deposit size. To see how deposit changes risk bands, use the Loan-to-Value (LTV) Calculator.

08

Should I use gross or net income?

Gross income is typically used for income multiple calculations. Net monthly income is more appropriate for payment-to-income checks as it reflects your actual take-home pay.

09

How do interest rates affect my borrowing power?

Higher rates mean higher monthly payments for the same loan amount, which reduces the maximum you can borrow. Even a 0.5% change can shift borrowing power significantly. You can stress-test rate moves further with the Interest Rate Change Calculator.

10

Should I speak to a mortgage broker?

If you are serious about buying, a broker can match you with the lender most likely to approve your application and secure the best rate for your circumstances.

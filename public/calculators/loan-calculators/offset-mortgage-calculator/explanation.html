How To Guide

How much interest can an offset mortgage save me?

An offset mortgage calculator estimates how much interest your savings balance eliminates from a mortgage over the full term, how many years you could pay off early, and how growing that savings pot month by month compounds those gains over time.

An offset account does not earn interest on savings. Instead, it reduces the balance the lender charges interest against. If your mortgage balance is 250,000 and your offset savings are 20,000, the lender only calculates interest on 230,000 each month. Your repayment stays the same, but less of each payment covers interest - so more attacks the principal. Over 25 years, that difference can grow into tens of thousands of pounds saved and years trimmed from the loan term.

Why use an offset mortgage calculator?

Many borrowers with linked savings accounts underestimate how powerful consistent offset savings can be. The interest saving is not just on the initial savings balance - it applies every single month for the remaining life of the loan, and it compounds further as monthly contributions grow the offset pot.

The calculator is useful for:

  • Testing whether a lump-sum savings deposit changes the payoff picture meaningfully
  • Comparing full and partial offset modes to understand conservative versus optimistic estimates
  • Modelling the long-term impact of committing a regular monthly amount to the offset account
  • Deciding between overpaying the mortgage directly and keeping savings accessible in an offset account

How does an offset mortgage calculator work?

The calculator runs two parallel scenarios and compares the results.

Step 1 - Baseline monthly payment

The fixed monthly repayment is calculated from the original mortgage balance, interest rate, and term. This payment does not change when offset savings are applied.

M = P x [r x (1 + r)^n] / [(1 + r)^n - 1]

Where:

  • M = monthly repayment
  • P = mortgage balance
  • r = monthly interest rate = annual rate / 12 / 100
  • n = total months remaining = years x 12

Step 2 - Effective balance each month

The effective balance is the amount the lender charges interest against after offset savings are applied. It changes each month as the offset pot grows through contributions.

Full Offset mode:

Effective Balance = Mortgage Balance - Offset Savings

Partial / Average mode (50% factor):

Effective Balance = Mortgage Balance - (0.5 x Offset Savings)

Partial mode applies 50% of the offset balance to represent the practical reality that a daily-averaging account rarely holds its maximum possible amount through the full month.

Step 3 - Monthly interest on effective balance

Offset Interest Charge = Effective Balance x r

Because the monthly repayment stays fixed but the interest portion falls, more of each payment reduces the principal. That in turn reduces next month's effective balance even further, creating a compounding cycle.

Step 4 - Interest saved and time saved

Interest Saved = Baseline Total Interest - Offset Total Interest

The calculator runs this month by month until the offset loan balance reaches zero, then compares the payoff month against the original term to calculate time saved.

What do the calculator inputs mean?

Mortgage Balance

The outstanding balance owed today, not the original loan amount. Use your latest lender statement figure. A higher balance gives more room for offset savings to reduce the interest-bearing amount.

Interest Rate (APR %)

The annual percentage rate on the mortgage. Offset savings are most valuable when rates are higher because the effective balance reduction shields a larger interest charge each month.

Remaining Term (years)

The number of years left on the mortgage. A longer remaining term means offset savings have more opportunities to reduce the interest charge and a bigger total saving is possible.

Offset Savings Balance

The current amount sitting in the linked offset account. This is the starting shield against interest. In Full Offset mode the entire balance applies; in Partial mode only half is counted.

Monthly Offset Contribution

The regular monthly deposit added to the offset account. Even modest contributions compound significantly over a 20- or 25-year mortgage term because the growing offset pot shields interest across an increasing share of the balance.

Offset Mode

  • Full Offset - the entire savings balance is offset against the mortgage balance each month. This applies when your lender credits the full account balance daily.
  • Partial / Average - 50% of the savings balance is applied. Use this for a more conservative estimate or when your account tracks a daily average that may be lower than the month-end balance.

What do the calculator outputs mean?

Monthly Payment (Baseline)

This is the standard amortisation payment. It stays the same throughout the offset scenario because offset accounts do not reduce the scheduled repayment - they reduce the interest portion within that repayment, so more of it clears principal.

Interest Saved

The cumulative gap between baseline interest paid and offset interest paid across the life of the loan. This is arguably the most important number because it shows what the offset account is actually worth in cash terms.

Time Saved

How many years and months earlier the loan pays off compared to the original schedule. This happens because reduced interest means each payment clears more principal, and the balance reaches zero sooner.

Effective Balance

The mortgage balance the lender uses to charge interest each month, after offset savings are applied. A lower effective balance is better: it means less of each payment is consumed by interest.

How to use the offset mortgage calculator

  1. Enter your current mortgage balance. Use your latest statement, not the original loan.
  2. Set the interest rate. Use the rate you are currently paying, as a percentage.
  3. Enter the remaining term. This should be years remaining, not the original term.
  4. Enter your current offset savings balance. Use the balance the account holds today.
  5. Add a monthly contribution. Even 100 or 200 per month makes a visible difference over a long term.
  6. Choose your offset mode. Full Offset for a best-case view; Partial / Average for a conservative estimate.
  7. Review the results. Look at Interest Saved, Time Saved, and the Amortization Table together - do not rely on any single figure.
  8. Adjust the savings balance and contribution. Test what happens if you move more savings into the account, or commit to a higher monthly deposit.

What Gross Margin Does Your Price Create?

Use this margin calculator when you know cost and selling price, or when you need to work backward from cost and a target margin percentage to a viable selling price. It is built for pricing decisions where protecting gross profit matters more than quoting one generic percentage result.

How to Guide

  1. Enter cost and selling price, or switch modes if you want to solve for another field.
  2. Check the profit amount first.
  3. Then review the margin percentage to see how much of the sale price is kept.
  4. Compare the result with your target margin before changing price.

How Margin Is Measured

This calculator measures gross margin from cost and price, or works backward when you want to know the selling price required for a target margin. In Cost + Price -> Margin % mode, a cost of $60.00 and selling price of $100.00 produce profit of $40.00 and gross margin of 40.00%.

((P - C) / P) x 100

Margin vs markup

Margin is based on selling price, not cost, which is why the same sale produces a different margin percentage than markup.

Reverse pricing

If a business manages price floors by margin, the reverse mode helps you work back to a required selling price instead of guessing.

Cost completeness

The result is only as reliable as the cost base behind it, so freight, packaging, payment fees, and routine discounting should not be ignored.

When to Use a Margin Calculator

  • Pricing decisions: Check whether a proposed selling price protects the gross margin target your business needs.
  • Discount planning: See how a lower selling price changes margin before you approve a promotion or negotiation.
  • Target-price planning: Work backward from cost and desired margin when you need a minimum acceptable price.
  • Margin versus markup checks: Confirm that a cost-based markup rule still lands at an acceptable margin percentage.

How to Read the Result

  • Profit amount: The money left after cost is removed from the selling price.
  • Margin percentage: The share of the selling price retained as gross profit.
  • Reverse pricing: Useful when you know cost and target margin but not the price.
  • Decision use: Strong for pricing, discount control, and gross-profit planning.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do you calculate gross margin?

Subtract cost from selling price, divide by selling price, then multiply by 100.

How do you find price from cost and target margin?

Divide cost by one minus the margin rate written as a decimal.

Is margin the same as markup?

No. Margin is based on selling price, while markup is based on cost.

Does the calculator show net profit?

No. It shows gross margin before overhead and other indirect expenses.

What is the most common mistake?

Using markup targets when the business is actually managing to a margin target.

What Selling Price or Markup Percentage Does Your Cost Create?

Markup adds an amount on top of cost, so it is a useful pricing tool whenever you want to move from cost into price or work backward from price into markup.

This calculator supports both single-product pricing and basket-level analysis across multiple products.

How to Guide

  1. Choose between single-product and basket mode.
  2. Select whether you want to calculate price from markup or markup from price.
  3. Enter the values for the active mode and click Calculate markup.
  4. Use the summary rows to review weighted totals when you are in basket mode.

At a Glance

  • Mode: Single Product — Cost → Price
  • Products: 1
  • Total cost: $50.00
  • Total price: $70.00
  • Total markup: $20.00
  • Basket markup: 40.00%

Worked Example

If cost is $50.00 and markup is 40.00%, the markup amount is $20.00. That gives a selling price of $70.00.

In basket mode, the calculator compares total price to total cost so the combined markup is weighted rather than averaged across rows.

Formula

P = C x (1 + M / 100)

When you work backward from selling price, markup percentage is calculated on the cost base, not the selling price.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is markup?

Markup is the amount added to cost to get the selling price.

How do you calculate price from cost and markup?

Multiply cost by one plus the markup rate written as a decimal.

How do you calculate markup from cost and price?

Divide price minus cost by cost, then multiply by 100.

What is basket markup?

Basket markup compares total price against total cost across multiple products.

Is markup the same as margin?

No. Markup is based on cost, while margin is based on selling price.

What happens if cost is zero?

Markup percentage becomes undefined because the formula would divide by zero.

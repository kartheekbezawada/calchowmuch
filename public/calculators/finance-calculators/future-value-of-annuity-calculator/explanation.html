How to Guide

1. Set the recurring contribution

Enter the equal amount you plan to add each period. This route is strongest when the contribution amount stays consistent.

2. Choose a growth assumption

Use the savings rate or expected investment return that best reflects the plan you are modeling. Small rate changes can compound meaningfully over longer periods.

3. Match the timing correctly

Switch between years and months so the period count matches your real schedule, then choose the compounding rhythm that fits the assumption behind the rate.

4. Compare ordinary and due timing

Ordinary annuity assumes each payment lands at the end of the period. Annuity due assumes it arrives at the beginning, which typically produces more growth.

Frequently Asked Questions

01

What is the future value of an annuity?

It is the total accumulated value of a series of regular payments at a future date after earning compound interest over time.

02

What is the difference between ordinary annuity and annuity due?

Ordinary annuities pay at the end of each period. Annuity due pays at the beginning, resulting in a higher future value because payments compound longer.

03

Why is the FV of annuity due higher?

Because each payment is invested one period earlier, it earns compound interest for a longer time than an ordinary annuity.

04

What interest rate should I use?

Use a rate reflecting expected investment returns, savings rates, or long-term growth assumptions relevant to your financial plan.

05

Is the future value of annuity useful for retirement planning?

Yes, it is commonly used to estimate how regular 401(k), IRA, or pension contributions will grow over your working years.

06

Can I use this for monthly contributions?

Yes, the calculator supports monthly, quarterly, semi-annual, and annual payment and compounding periods.

07

What happens if the interest rate is zero?

If the rate is zero, the future value equals the total of all payments because no interest is earned and the annuity factor equals the number of periods.

08

How does compounding frequency affect results?

More frequent compounding, such as monthly instead of annual, increases the future value because interest is reinvested more often within each year.

09

Is this the same as a simple savings calculator?

It is similar but specifically models equal periodic payments. A savings calculator may include an initial lump sum as well.

10

Can I use FVA for investment planning?

Yes, it is widely used to evaluate savings plans, SIP investments, education funds, and long-term financial goals.

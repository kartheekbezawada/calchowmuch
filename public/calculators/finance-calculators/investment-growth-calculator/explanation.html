How Much Could This Portfolio Grow?

This calculator projects how a starting balance and recurring contributions may grow over time under a fixed return assumption.

Use the future value first, then compare total contributions and total gains to see how much of the ending balance comes from your deposits versus growth.

How to Guide

1. Start with the balance you have today

Enter the amount already invested or saved before any new contributions are added.

2. Use a realistic expected return

This is a planning assumption, not a promise. Conservative assumptions are usually more useful than optimistic ones.

3. Add contributions only if they are repeatable

Recurring deposits can materially raise the future value because each contribution also has time to compound.

4. Use inflation when you care about purchasing power

Inflation-adjusted output is often the better view when planning for future spending needs.

Frequently Asked Questions

01

What is an investment growth calculator?

It projects how an investment may grow using a return assumption, time horizon, and optional recurring deposits.

02

What matters most?

Time horizon, return assumption, and contribution consistency are usually the biggest drivers.

03

Why do contributions matter so much?

Each recurring deposit adds more capital and can also earn returns for the rest of the plan.

04

Does compounding frequency matter?

Yes. More frequent compounding generally produces a slightly higher ending balance under the same nominal return.

05

What if the return rate is 0%?

The future value becomes the starting amount plus total contributions.

06

Why include inflation?

It shows what the ending balance may be worth in today’s purchasing power, not just nominal dollars.

07

Is this result guaranteed?

No. It is a deterministic estimate based on fixed assumptions.

08

Can I use this for savings goals?

Yes. It works well for simple long-term savings and investment planning.

09

Why can small return changes create big differences?

Because they apply over every period and compound over time.

10

Should I trust the inflation-adjusted result more?

Use nominal and inflation-adjusted views together depending on whether you care about account value or real buying power.

Quick Answer Table

Rule Identity What the route confirms
Product log_b(xy) = log_b(x) + log_b(y) The log of a product becomes a sum of logs.
Quotient log_b(x / y) = log_b(x) - log_b(y) The log of a quotient becomes a difference of logs.
Power log_b(x^n) = n · log_b(x) The exponent moves out front as a multiplier.

Formula Guide

The product, quotient, and power rules all come from the same base idea: logarithms turn multiplication into addition, division into subtraction, and exponents into multipliers.

These identities only work cleanly when the base is valid and the log arguments stay positive. The route enforces those domain rules first, then shows the numeric output and the symbolic form together so the algebra stays easy to verify.

FAQ

What does the product rule do?

It turns the log of a product into the sum of two logs with the same base.

What does the quotient rule do?

It turns the log of a quotient into the difference of two logs with the same base.

What does the power rule do?

It moves the exponent in front of the logarithm as a multiplier.

Do these rules work for any base?

Yes, as long as the base is positive and not equal to 1.

Do the arguments need to be positive?

Yes. For real-number logs, the argument values must stay positive.

Why does the base matter?

The base changes the numeric output even though the algebraic identities stay the same.

Can I use decimals?

Yes. Positive decimal inputs work for the rule explorer too.

What if the base is 1?

The route rejects it because base 1 does not define a valid logarithm.

Is this solving symbolic expressions?

No. It numerically verifies the core identities so you can check your algebra faster.

When is this route useful?

It is useful for algebra review, precalculus homework checks, and quick log-rule verification.

Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)

ANOVA is a statistical method used to compare means across multiple groups to determine if at least one group mean is significantly different from the others.

One-Way ANOVA

Tests whether there are significant differences between the means of three or more independent groups.

Hypotheses

  • H0 (Null): All group means are equal (mu1 = mu2 = ... = muk)
  • H1 (Alternative): At least one group mean differs

Key Components

Sum of Squares

  • SSB (Between Groups): Variability due to differences between group means
  • SSW (Within Groups): Variability within each group (error)
  • SST (Total): Total variability in the data (SST = SSB + SSW)

Mean Squares

  • MSB: SSB / (k - 1), where k is the number of groups
  • MSW: SSW / (N - k), where N is the total sample size

F-Statistic

F = MSB / MSW

A larger F-statistic indicates greater between-group variability relative to within-group variability, suggesting group means differ.

Effect Sizes

Eta-Squared (eta2)

The proportion of total variance explained by group membership: eta2 = SSB / SST

  • eta2 >= 0.14: Large effect
  • eta2 >= 0.06: Medium effect
  • eta2 >= 0.01: Small effect

Omega-Squared (omega2)

A less biased estimate of effect size, especially for small samples.

Assumptions

  1. Independence: Observations are independent
  2. Normality: Data in each group are approximately normally distributed
  3. Homogeneity of Variance: Variances are equal across groups

Interpretation

If p-value < alpha (significance level), reject the null hypothesis and conclude that at least one group mean differs significantly. Follow up with post-hoc tests (like Tukey HSD) to identify which specific groups differ.

Correlation Analysis

Correlation measures the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two variables. The correlation coefficient ranges from -1 to +1.

Types of Correlation

Pearson Correlation (r)

Measures the linear relationship between two continuous variables.

r = sum((x - x-bar)(y - y-bar)) / sqrt(sum((x - x-bar)2) x sum((y - y-bar)2))

Assumes both variables are normally distributed and the relationship is linear.

Spearman Rank Correlation (rho)

A non-parametric measure based on ranks rather than actual values.

Appropriate when data are ordinal or when the relationship is monotonic but not linear.

Kendall's Tau (tau)

Another rank-based correlation that measures ordinal association.

More robust than Spearman's for small samples and handles ties better.

Interpreting Correlation Strength

|r| ValueInterpretation
0.9 - 1.0Very Strong
0.7 - 0.9Strong
0.5 - 0.7Moderate
0.3 - 0.5Weak
0.0 - 0.3Very Weak / No correlation

Direction

  • Positive correlation (r > 0): As X increases, Y tends to increase
  • Negative correlation (r < 0): As X increases, Y tends to decrease
  • Zero correlation (r = 0): No linear relationship

Coefficient of Determination (r2)

The square of the correlation coefficient represents the proportion of variance in Y that can be explained by X.

For example, r = 0.8 means r2 = 0.64, so 64% of the variance in Y is explained by X.

Significance Testing

The t-test for correlation tests whether r significantly differs from zero.

t = r x sqrt((n-2) / (1-r2))

A small p-value indicates the correlation is statistically significant.

Confidence Interval

Uses Fisher's z-transformation to construct confidence intervals for the correlation coefficient.

Important Notes

  • Correlation does not imply causation
  • Outliers can strongly influence correlation
  • Always visualize data with scatter plots
  • Sample size affects significance - large samples may show significant but weak correlations

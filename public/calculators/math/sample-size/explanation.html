Sample Size Planning Practical Guide

Use this calculator to plan a study sample for proportions or means, review finite-population correction when the research population is known, and document the assumptions behind the final target.

How to Guide

  1. Choose Proportion study for percentages, prevalence, response rates, or shares.
  2. Choose Mean study for continuous outcomes when the population standard deviation is known.
  3. Select the confidence level, enter the margin of error, and add the study-specific input for the active mode.
  4. Add population size only when the full research population is known and not very large.
  5. Click Calculate Sample Size to review the required sample, assumptions, and step-by-step derivation.

FAQ

When should I use the proportion mode?

Use proportion mode when the study outcome is a percentage, prevalence, response rate, or any other proportion.

When should I use the mean mode?

Use mean mode when the study outcome is continuous and you know the population standard deviation in the same units as the margin of error.

Why does the calculator default to 50% for the estimated proportion?

A 50% proportion gives the largest variance term and therefore the most conservative sample-size estimate when prior evidence is limited.

What does finite-population correction do?

It reduces the required sample size when the full population is known and relatively small compared with the uncorrected sample-size estimate.

Do I need a population size for every study?

No. Leave population size blank for large or effectively infinite populations and the calculator will use the standard uncorrected formula.

Why is the final sample size rounded up?

The final answer is rounded up so the achieved sample is at least as large as the planned requirement.

What happens if I tighten the margin of error?

A tighter margin of error requires a larger sample size because the estimate must be more precise.

What happens if I increase the confidence level?

Higher confidence levels use larger z-values, which increases the required sample size.

Can this replace a full power analysis?

No. This calculator is for estimation planning, not for hypothesis-test power analysis or multi-arm experimental design.

Does the calculator store my study inputs?

No. The calculation runs in your browser and does not store the values you enter.

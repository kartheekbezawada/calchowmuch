What is Probability?

Probability measures the likelihood of an event occurring, expressed as a number between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain), or equivalently 0% to 100%.

When to Use This Calculator

  • Calculating odds of combined events
  • Risk assessment and decision making
  • Game theory and gambling odds
  • Quality control probability calculations
  • Understanding basic probability rules

Operations Available

  • Single Event P(A): The basic probability of event A.
  • Complement: The probability of A NOT happening, equal to 1 - P(A).
  • AND (Independent): The probability of both A and B occurring when they are independent.
  • OR (Independent): The probability of at least one of A or B occurring when they are independent.

Input Definitions

  • P(A): The probability of event A occurring, as a percentage (0-100%).
  • P(B): The probability of event B occurring, as a percentage (0-100%). Only needed for AND/OR operations.

Formulas

Single Event: P(A) = probability as decimal

Complement: P(not A) = 1 - P(A)

AND (Independent): P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)

OR (Independent): P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A) × P(B)

Worked Example (AND)

Using defaults: P(A) = 30%, P(B) = 50%

  1. Convert to decimals: P(A) = 0.30, P(B) = 0.50
  2. Apply formula: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)
  3. Calculate: 0.30 × 0.50 = 0.15
  4. Result: 0.15 or 15%

There is a 15% chance that both events occur.

Worked Example (OR)

Using: P(A) = 30%, P(B) = 50%

  1. Convert to decimals: P(A) = 0.30, P(B) = 0.50
  2. Apply formula: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A) × P(B)
  3. Calculate: 0.30 + 0.50 - (0.30 × 0.50) = 0.80 - 0.15 = 0.65
  4. Result: 0.65 or 65%

There is a 65% chance that at least one event occurs.

Worked Example (Complement)

Using: P(A) = 30%

  1. Convert to decimal: P(A) = 0.30
  2. Apply formula: P(not A) = 1 - P(A)
  3. Calculate: 1 - 0.30 = 0.70
  4. Result: 0.70 or 70%

There is a 70% chance that event A does NOT occur.

Important Notes

  • Independence: These formulas assume events are independent (one event doesn't affect the other).
  • For dependent events, you need conditional probability formulas not covered here.
  • The OR formula accounts for the overlap - we subtract P(A)×P(B) to avoid counting it twice.

Edge Cases and Validation

  • All probabilities must be between 0% and 100%.
  • P(A) = 0% means the event never occurs.
  • P(A) = 100% means the event always occurs.
  • Results are shown as both decimal and percentage.

Unit Circle Overview

The unit circle is a circle with radius 1 centered at the origin. Every angle corresponds to a point (x, y) on the circle where x = cos(angle) and y = sin(angle). This makes the unit circle the most direct way to visualize trigonometric values.

Quadrants and Signs

Quadrants determine the sign of sine and cosine: Quadrant I (+,+), Quadrant II (-,+), Quadrant III (-,-), Quadrant IV (+,-). Tangent follows the sign of sin/cos.

Reference Angles

A reference angle is the acute angle between the terminal side and the x-axis. It helps match any angle to a special triangle so you can recover exact values.

Special Angles

The angles 0, 30, 45, 60, 90 (and their quadrant reflections) have exact values like 1/2, sqrt(2)/2, and sqrt(3)/2. The calculator shows both exact and decimal values.

SOHCAHTOA Reminder

In right triangles: sin = opposite/hypotenuse, cos = adjacent/hypotenuse, tan = opposite/adjacent. The unit circle makes the hypotenuse 1, so sin and cos are the y- and x-coordinates directly.

Applications

Unit circle values appear in physics (waves), engineering (rotations), navigation, and computer graphics. Memorizing special angles speeds up many real-world calculations.

How to Guide

When to Use a Loan Comparison Calculator

Use this page when you already have two borrowing scenarios and need to see which one costs less overall. That could mean comparing two lenders, a short term against a long term, or a refinance offer against the current loan you already have.

The key benefit is that it separates payment affordability from true borrowing cost. A loan can look cheaper each month while still costing more over the full term.

How to Enter the Two Loan Offers

Enter the amount, APR, and term for Loan A, then do the same for Loan B. Each loan is amortized independently and then combined into one comparison table and one shared payoff schedule.

  • Loan amount is the principal borrowed for that offer.
  • APR is the annual borrowing rate used to estimate interest.
  • Term sets how many months or years the repayment runs.

What the Combined Payment Means

The combined monthly payment adds the monthly repayment from Loan A and Loan B for the same period. If one loan ends earlier, later rows show only the remaining loan payment.

This is useful for cash-flow planning because it shows not only which offer costs less, but also how long the higher payment burden lasts.

How to Compare the Better Offer

Read the comparison table across four outputs: monthly payment, total interest, total paid, and payoff horizon. Those outputs answer slightly different questions, so choose the one that matches your actual goal.

Worked Example

Imagine Loan A is a lower APR offer over a shorter term, while Loan B stretches repayment over longer years to reduce the monthly bill. Loan B may look more comfortable each month, but the longer term can create more total interest even if the payment looks easier.

This calculator makes that trade-off visible immediately by showing both affordability and lifetime cost in the same view.

How Input Changes Affect the Outcome

  • Reducing APR usually lowers total interest more directly than extending term.
  • Extending term usually lowers monthly payment but can raise total paid.
  • Changing only one loan lets you see whether refinancing that offer helps overall.
  • Two similar monthly payments can still have very different lifetime costs.

Common Comparison Mistakes

  • Choosing a loan using monthly payment alone.
  • Ignoring how much longer one offer keeps debt outstanding.
  • Assuming a smaller monthly payment always means a better deal.
  • Comparing offers with different principal amounts as if they were identical products.

Frequently Asked Questions

01

What does this multiple car loan calculator compare?

It compares two auto loans at once and shows each payment, each total interest, and the combined repayment totals.

02

How is the combined monthly payment calculated?

It adds Loan A monthly payment and Loan B monthly payment for each month.

03

How is combined total interest calculated?

It adds total interest from both amortization schedules across their full terms.

04

Can the two loans have different terms and APRs?

Yes. Each loan has independent amount, APR, and term inputs.

05

What does payoff horizon mean in this view?

Payoff horizon is the longer of the two loan terms, which is when the combined balance reaches zero.

06

Why does the monthly total drop later in the schedule?

If one loan ends earlier, only the remaining loan payment is left for later months.

07

Does this include taxes, fees, or insurance?

No. This calculator models principal and interest only for each loan using fixed-rate amortization.

08

Can this help decide whether to refinance one loan?

Yes. You can change one loan APR or term and compare the new combined totals against the previous scenario.

09

Is this result lender-specific?

No. It is a general estimate using fixed-rate math, so exact lender disclosures may differ.

10

Can I view monthly and yearly payoff summaries?

Yes. Use the table toggle to switch between detailed monthly rows and yearly rollup rows.

Important Notes

  • Results are estimates based on fixed-rate amortization, not lender disclosures.
  • This comparison models principal and interest only unless other costs are built into the amounts entered.
  • If one loan has fees, insurance, or add-on products outside principal, compare those separately.
  • Displayed values are rounded, so exact lender schedules can differ slightly.
  • Use this tool for planning and offer comparison, not as financial advice.

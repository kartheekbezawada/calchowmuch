Quick Answer Table

Formula Notes

The slope uses m = (y2 - y1) / (x2 - x1). The distance uses d = sqrt((x2 - x1)^2 + (y2 - y1)^2), and the midpoint uses ((x1 + x2) / 2, (y1 + y2) / 2).

When x2 - x1 = 0, the line is vertical and the slope is undefined. When y2 - y1 = 0, the line is horizontal and the slope is zero.

Worked Example

Diagonal line

Use Point 1 (1, 2) and Point 2 (4, 8).

The slope is 2, the midpoint is (2.5, 5), and the distance is about 6.708. This is a general line because both coordinates change.

Vertical line

Use Point 1 (3, 1) and Point 2 (3, 9).

The distance is 8, the midpoint is (3, 5), and the slope is undefined because the line runs straight up and down.

FAQ

What does slope tell me?

Slope tells you how much the line rises or falls for each one-unit change in x.

Why is a vertical line undefined?

A vertical line makes the x-change equal to zero, so the slope formula would divide by zero.

How is midpoint calculated?

Midpoint averages the two x-values and the two y-values separately.

Does distance come from the Pythagorean theorem?

Yes. The distance formula is the coordinate-plane version of the Pythagorean theorem.

Can I use decimal coordinates?

Yes. The calculator accepts decimal and negative coordinates.

What line forms are included?

The detail panel shows slope-intercept, point-slope, standard, parallel, and perpendicular forms when applicable.

What happens on a horizontal line?

The slope becomes zero and the equation is shown as y = constant.

Can both points be the same?

Yes, but the distance becomes zero and the line relationship is degenerate.

Does the calculator show parallel and perpendicular references?

Yes. It shows representative parallel and perpendicular equations through Point 1.

Why can standard form look different from my textbook?

Equivalent standard forms can be scaled or signed differently while still representing the same line.

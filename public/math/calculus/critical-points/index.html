Formula Guide

A critical point usually appears where f'(x) = 0 or where the derivative does not exist. To classify the point, the route checks the sign of the first derivative on each side and also estimates the second derivative. A positive second derivative suggests a local minimum, while a negative second derivative suggests a local maximum.

Inflection points are different. They appear where the curve changes concavity, which is why the route also scans for sign changes in the second-derivative estimate.

FAQ

What is a critical point?

It is a point where the derivative is zero or undefined, making it a candidate turning point.

Is every critical point a maximum or minimum?

No. Some critical points are flat points or inconclusive points instead of true extrema.

What is the difference between a critical point and an inflection point?

A critical point focuses on slope behavior, while an inflection point focuses on concavity changing direction.

Why does the range matter?

The route only searches inside the chosen window, so a narrow range can miss turning points outside it.

Why does the route use numerical checks?

It uses numerical derivative estimates to keep the route fast for quick practical review instead of full symbolic calculus.

Can I change the variable name?

Yes. The route now evaluates the function using the variable you enter, such as x, t, or y.

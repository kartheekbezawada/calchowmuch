Integral Calculator Practical Guide

Use this integral calculator to switch between antiderivatives and definite integral values in a clean answer-first layout. It is built for polynomial-style expressions, so the route keeps the integrated result, bounds summary, and rule-by-rule steps together instead of hiding them in a legacy shell.

  • Best for: quick antiderivatives, definite-area checks, and power-rule practice.
  • Quick answer flow: enter the function, choose the mode, then calculate once.
  • Output includes: the antiderivative, definite value when requested, and step-by-step notes.

How to Guide

  1. Enter a polynomial-style expression such as x^2 + 3*x + 5.
  2. Choose Indefinite when you want the antiderivative family.
  3. Choose Definite and enter lower and upper bounds when you want a numeric value.
  4. Click Calculate integral and read the answer card before reviewing the steps panel.

Formula Guide

The main rule in this route is the power rule for integration: ∫x^n dx = x^(n+1)/(n+1) + C when n ≠ -1. Constant terms turn into linear terms, and polynomial-style expressions integrate term by term.

In definite mode, the route uses the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus: ∫[a to b]f(x)dx = F(b) - F(a). That is why the answer card shows both the numeric value and the antiderivative used to evaluate it.

Worked Example

Indefinite example

Enter x^2 and keep the mode on Indefinite.

The route returns 0.3333x^3 + C, which is the decimal form of x³/3 + C.

Definite example

Use the same function, switch to Definite, and keep the bounds at 0 and 1.

The route returns about 0.333333 because F(1) - F(0) = 1/3.

FAQ

What is an indefinite integral?

It is the family of antiderivatives of the input function, so the answer includes + C.

What is a definite integral?

It is the numeric accumulated change between two bounds.

Why does the indefinite answer include C?

Different constants disappear under differentiation, so every antiderivative differs by a constant.

What kind of expressions work best here?

Polynomial-style expressions with one main variable work best in this route.

What do the bounds mean?

They define the interval where the definite integral is evaluated.

Why does definite mode still show the antiderivative?

The numeric answer comes from evaluating the antiderivative at the upper and lower bounds.

Can the definite result be negative?

Yes. If the net signed area is negative over the chosen interval, the result can be negative.

Does this route handle full symbolic integration?

No. It is designed for quick polynomial-style integration rather than a full CAS engine.

What happens if I switch modes?

The answer card updates to either the antiderivative family or the bound-based numeric value.

When is this route useful?

It is useful for calculus practice, quick antiderivative checks, and estimating accumulated change.

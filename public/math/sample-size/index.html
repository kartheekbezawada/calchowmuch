Sample Size Calculator Complete Practical Guide

Use this sample size calculator to plan proportion or mean studies with a defensible target before data collection starts. It keeps the required sample, formulas, worked examples, and decision notes in one place so you can brief a survey, coursework project, or research plan without digging through the full guide first.

How to Guide

  1. Choose Proportion study for percentages, prevalence, response rates, and other shares.
  2. Choose Mean study for continuous outcomes when you can justify a population standard deviation.
  3. Pick the confidence level and enter the margin of error that matches the precision you need.
  4. Enter the mode-specific planning input: estimated proportion for proportion studies or sigma for mean studies.
  5. Add a population size only when the full population is known and small enough for finite-population correction to matter.
  6. Click Calculate Sample Size and review the required sample, interpretation paragraph, assumptions, and derivation steps.
  7. Use the sensitivity table to judge whether a slightly looser or tighter margin is realistic for time, budget, and recruitment constraints.

All Formulas Used on This Page

Proportion sample size for an effectively infinite population

n0 = z^2 x p x (1 - p) / E^2

Use this when the study outcome is a percentage or prevalence and the total population is large enough that finite-population correction is not needed.

Variable reference table for the proportion sample size formula
Variable Meaning
z Z-score for the chosen confidence level: 1.645, 1.960, or 2.576.
p Estimated proportion as a decimal.
E Margin of error as a decimal.
n0 Base sample size before finite-population correction.

Mean sample size for an effectively infinite population

n0 = (z x sigma / E)^2

Use this when the outcome is continuous and the standard deviation is known or supported by pilot data, prior studies, or domain evidence.

Variable reference table for the mean sample size formula
Variable Meaning
z Z-score for the chosen confidence level.
sigma Population standard deviation in the same units as the outcome.
E Margin of error in the same units as sigma.
n0 Base sample size before any correction for a finite cohort.

Finite-population correction

n = n0 / (1 + (n0 - 1) / N)

Apply this only when the full population size N is known and the planned sample would otherwise represent a meaningful share of that population.

What Does the Sample Size Calculator Tell Me?

The calculator tells you how many observations you need so the final estimate lands within a chosen margin of error at a chosen confidence level. That planning target is what you use to size recruitment, fieldwork, incentives, and data-collection effort.

Bigger samples increase precision because the margin of error shrinks. Higher confidence also increases the sample because the interval must be wide enough to cover the true population value more often.

The page also shows the assumptions behind the number. That matters because a sample-size estimate is only defensible when the mode, variance assumption, and population logic all fit the study design.

Finally, the sensitivity table helps you negotiate tradeoffs. It shows how the requirement shifts when you tighten or relax the margin of error while everything else stays fixed.

Worked Examples

Worked example 1

General survey baseline. Use 95% confidence, a 5% margin, and p = 50%.

The base formula gives n0 = 384.16, which rounds up to 385 responses.

Worked example 2

Conservative p = 50% planning. Tighten the margin to 4% while keeping 95% confidence.

The sample rises to 601, showing how fast precision increases the recruitment burden.

Worked example 3

Finite-population survey. A campus survey uses 95% confidence, a 5% margin, p = 50%, and N = 1200.

The uncorrected n0 is 384.16, but finite-population correction lowers the final target to 292.

Worked example 4

Prevalence planning. Assume 20% prevalence and a 3% margin at 95% confidence.

The study needs 683 observations because tighter precision still dominates even with a lower prevalence.

Worked example 5

Mean-estimate laboratory study. Use mean mode with 95% confidence, sigma = 8, and margin = 2 units.

The base calculation gives n0 = 61.47, so the study should plan for 62 observations.

Worked example 6

Small-cohort mean estimate. Keep the same mean-study assumptions and add N = 180.

Finite correction lowers the sample target to 47, which is more realistic for a bounded cohort.

FAQ

When should I use the proportion study mode?

Use proportion study mode when the outcome is a percentage, response rate, prevalence, share, or other binary proportion.

When should I use the mean study mode?

Use mean study mode when the outcome is continuous and the standard deviation is known or can be justified from prior evidence.

Why does the calculator default to 50% for the estimated proportion?

A 50% planning proportion maximizes the variance term and therefore produces the most conservative sample-size estimate.

What does finite-population correction do?

It reduces the sample requirement when the total population is known and the uncorrected sample would otherwise cover a large share of that population.

Do I always need to enter a population size?

No. Leave it blank for large or effectively infinite populations and the calculator will use the standard uncorrected formula.

Why is the final sample size rounded up?

Rounding up ensures the planned study always meets or exceeds the requested level of precision.

What happens when I tighten the margin of error?

The sample requirement increases, often sharply, because tighter precision is more demanding.

What happens when I increase the confidence level?

The z-score increases, which pushes the required sample size higher.

Can this replace a full power analysis?

No. This calculator supports estimation planning and does not replace power calculations for effect detection or experimental design.

Does the calculator store my study inputs?

No. The calculation runs in your browser and does not save the values you enter.

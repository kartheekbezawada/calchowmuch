Correlation Calculator Practical Guide

Use this route to measure whether two variables move together, how strongly they move together, and whether that relationship is statistically meaningful. It keeps the coefficient, strength label, p-value, and confidence interval in one answer-first panel so you can review the relationship before fitting a regression line.

  • Best for: quick relationship checks between paired numeric or ranked variables.
  • Quick answer flow: paste paired lists, choose the coefficient family, and read the answer card first.
  • Output includes: coefficient, interpretation, significance test, and confidence interval when available.

How to Guide

  1. Paste paired X and Y values with the same number of data points.
  2. Use Pearson for linear continuous relationships and rank-based options for monotonic relationships.
  3. Choose an alpha level that matches how strict you want the significance test to be.
  4. Click Calculate correlation and compare strength, direction, and p-value together.

Formula Guide

Pearson correlation compares centred X and Y values and scales them by their spread. Spearman and Kendall shift the focus to ranks and ordered pairs. In plain terms, the route is asking whether larger X values tend to come with larger Y values, smaller Y values, or no clear pattern at all.

FAQ

What does a negative correlation mean?

It means larger X values tend to come with smaller Y values, so the relationship slopes downward.

Does correlation prove causation?

No. Correlation only shows association, not that one variable directly causes the other.

When should I use Spearman instead of Pearson?

Use Spearman when the values are better understood by ranks or the relationship is monotonic but not clearly linear.

Why might Kendall be useful?

Kendall is often more stable with smaller samples or data that contains many tied ranks.

What makes a correlation statistically significant?

A small p-value relative to your chosen alpha suggests the observed association is unlikely to be zero in the source population.

Why should I still inspect the data visually?

Outliers or curved patterns can distort a coefficient, so a scatter plot can reveal problems the summary number hides.

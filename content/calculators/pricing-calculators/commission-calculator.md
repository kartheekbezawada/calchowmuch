# Commission Calculator

This calculator estimates how much commission a sale generates under either a flat rate or a tiered structure. It is designed for compensation planning, brokerage checks, affiliate payout reviews, and sales forecasting where the practical question is simple: what will this deal actually pay?

## How to Guide

1. Choose flat rate or tiered mode.
2. Enter total sales and the matching rate or tier structure.
3. Review total commission, effective rate, and any tier breakdown.
4. Re-run the calculation if you want to compare different deal sizes or plan designs.

## How Commission Is Calculated

Flat commission is the straightforward version. You take total sales and apply one commission percentage to the full amount. If a plan pays 7.5% on $30,000 of sales, the commission is $2,250. That model is common in affiliate programs, referral deals, and smaller sales teams where the payout rules are easy to audit.

Tiered commission is different. Each band of sales earns its own rate, and only the sales inside that band receive that rate. That means the highest tier does not automatically apply to the full deal. This is the point many people miss when reading compensation plans. A top rate may look attractive on paper, but the effective rate across the full sale can still be much lower once lower bands are included.

The effective commission rate is often the most useful output because it converts the whole payout into one percentage of total sales. That makes it easier to compare a tiered offer with a flat offer, or to test whether a threshold change actually improves the plan in a meaningful way.

Formula:

- Flat commission: Commission = Sales x (Rate / 100)
- Effective rate: Total commission / Total sales x 100
- Tiered commission: Sum of the commission earned in each sales band

## What the Result Means

Use the result as a base payout estimate, not as a complete payroll model. Real-world commission plans often depend on more than one percentage field.

- Sales basis matters: some plans pay on gross revenue, others on net revenue, margin, bookings, or collected cash.
- Timing matters: some businesses pay at contract signature, others only after cash is received.
- Structure matters: split deals, overlays, managers, and house accounts can reduce the payout that one person receives.
- Plan rules matter: caps, clawbacks, accelerators, minimum thresholds, and quota gates can all change the final number.

If your plan contains those conditions, use this calculator to estimate the core commission first, then apply the policy rules on top.

## Example Scenario

Assume a rep closes $90,000 under a three-tier structure:

- 5% on the first $25,000
- 7% on the next $35,000
- 10% on the amount above $60,000

The commission is built in layers:

- First $25,000 at 5% = $1,250
- Next $35,000 at 7% = $2,450
- Final $30,000 at 10% = $3,000

Total commission is $6,700. The top rate is 10%, but the effective rate is about 7.44%. That is the number to use if you are comparing this plan against a flat commission alternative.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating the highest tier as if it applies to the whole sale
- Entering gross sales when the plan actually pays on net sales
- Ignoring split commissions on shared deals
- Comparing headline rates instead of effective rates
- Forgetting that quarterly and annual plans can produce very different outcomes

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do you calculate flat commission?

Multiply total sales by the commission rate written as a decimal.

### How do tiered commissions work?

Each band of sales earns its own rate, and the band-level payouts are added together.

### What is the effective commission rate?

It is total commission divided by total sales, shown as a percentage of the full sale.

### Can I use this for real estate, affiliate, or brokerage payouts?

Yes. It works for any arrangement that uses flat rates or progressive rate bands.

### Does the calculator include caps or clawbacks?

No. It calculates the mathematical payout from the entered sales and rate structure only.

### What is the most common mistake?

Treating the highest tier as if it applies to the full sale instead of only the sales inside that band.

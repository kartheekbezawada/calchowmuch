# Discount Calculator

This calculator converts a percentage discount into the two figures that matter at checkout: the amount saved and the final price paid. It is useful for shopping comparisons, promotional pricing, coupon validation, and quick retail checks where headline percentages can be misleading.

## How to Guide

1. Enter the original price.
2. Enter the discount percentage.
3. Review the discount amount, savings, and final price.
4. Repeat with other offers if you want to compare deals properly.

## How the Discount Works

The calculator starts with the original price, calculates the discount amount, and subtracts that amount from the starting price. That sounds simple, but it solves one of the most common shopping mistakes: comparing discount percentages without comparing the actual price after the discount is applied.

A 40% discount does not automatically mean a better deal than 25% off. If the original price is much higher, the final price can still end up worse. That is why the final price is usually the decision number, while the savings amount is the context number.

Formula:

- Discount amount = Original price x (Discount / 100)
- Final price = Original price - Discount amount

## What to Compare When You Shop

If you are choosing between two offers, compare them in this order:

- Final price: the amount you actually pay
- Savings amount: how much is being removed
- Starting price: whether the discount is based on a realistic pre-sale price
- Extra costs: tax, delivery, fees, and returns policy

This matters because some retailers use inflated reference prices or mixed offers that look stronger than they are. A lower discount on a lower base price can still be the cheaper transaction.

Stacked discounts also need care. In most cases, two discounts are applied one after another rather than added together. That means 20% off plus another 10% off does not equal 30% off the original price. The second reduction is taken from an already discounted number.

## Example Scenario

Store A sells an item for $120 with 25% off. Store B sells a similar item for $150 with 35% off.

- Store A final price: $120 - $30 = $90
- Store B final price: $150 - $52.50 = $97.50

Store B advertises the larger percentage discount, but Store A still produces the lower final price. That is exactly why percentage alone is not enough.

## Common Mistakes

- Comparing discount percentages instead of final prices
- Using an inflated reference price as the original price
- Forgetting that stacked discounts usually apply in sequence
- Ignoring tax, shipping, or minimum-order rules
- Confusing amount saved with amount paid

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do you calculate the final price after a discount?

Multiply the original price by one minus the discount rate as a decimal.

### Is the discount amount the same as savings?

Yes. They are two labels for the same reduction amount.

### Do multiple discounts add together?

Usually no. They are normally applied one after another, not added together.

### Does the calculator include sales tax?

No. It shows the discounted price before tax.

### Can I use decimal discounts like 12.5%?

Yes. Decimal percentage discounts are handled normally.

### What is the most common mistake?

Comparing percentage discounts without comparing the final prices.

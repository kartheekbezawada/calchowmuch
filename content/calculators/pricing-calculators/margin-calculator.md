# Margin Calculator

This calculator shows gross margin from cost and selling price, or works backward to find the selling price required to reach a target margin. It is built for pricing decisions, profitability checks, supplier reviews, and any business case where margin discipline matters more than guesswork.

## How to Guide

1. Choose whether you want margin from cost and price or price from cost and target margin.
2. Enter the cost and the matching second input for that mode.
3. Review gross margin, profit, and the required selling price.
4. Re-run the numbers to test different costs, prices, or margin targets.

## How Margin Is Measured

Gross margin is the share of selling price left after direct cost is removed. If a product sells for $100 and costs $60, the profit is $40 and the gross margin is 40%. The key point is that margin is measured against the selling price, not against cost.

That is why margin and markup are not the same number. Markup measures how much is added on top of cost. Margin measures how much of revenue remains after cost. In pricing work, using the wrong one can create serious underpricing or unrealistic targets.

Formula:

- Gross margin = ((Selling price - Cost) / Selling price) x 100
- Profit = Selling price - Cost
- Target selling price = Cost / (1 - Margin rate)

## How to Read the Result

The result is strongest when the cost input is complete. If freight, packaging, payment processing, or routine discounting are excluded, the reported margin will be overstated. For that reason, margin decisions should be based on net effective selling price and full direct cost wherever possible.

This calculator shows gross margin, not net profit. It does not automatically include rent, salaries, tax, financing, or channel fees unless you build those into cost. That makes it useful for pricing control and product screening, but not for replacing a full profit and loss model.

Use the result in three practical ways:

- To test whether a current price still supports the margin you need
- To work backward from a required margin to a minimum price
- To see how much margin is lost when cost rises or discounting increases

## Example Scenario

Assume a product costs $48 and the business requires a 55% gross margin.

- Required price = $48 / (1 - 0.55)
- Required price = $48 / 0.45
- Required price = $106.67

If the same product is actually being sold for $95, the margin falls below target. That does not necessarily mean the price is wrong, but it does mean the business is accepting less room for overhead and profit than planned.

## Common Mistakes

- Confusing margin with markup
- Using list price instead of real transaction price
- Leaving out direct variable costs
- Treating gross margin as net profit
- Setting target margins without checking market pricing

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do you calculate gross margin?

Subtract cost from selling price, divide by selling price, then multiply by 100.

### How do you find price from cost and target margin?

Divide cost by one minus the margin rate written as a decimal.

### Is margin the same as markup?

No. Margin is based on selling price, while markup is based on cost, so the percentages differ.

### Why does discounting matter so much?

Because even a small fall in selling price can remove a large portion of profit while cost stays fixed.

### Does the calculator show net profit?

No. It shows gross margin before overhead and other indirect expenses.

### What is the most common mistake?

Using markup targets when the business actually requires margin targets.

# Markup Calculator

This calculator moves between cost, selling price, and markup percentage for a single product or a basket of products. It is useful for retail pricing, quoting, catalog reviews, and basket-level checks where you need to understand how much is being added on top of cost.

## How to Guide

1. Choose single-product or basket mode.
2. Choose cost-to-price or price-to-markup mode.
3. Enter the values for the active setup.
4. Review selling price, markup amount, and basket markup if multiple products are included.

## How Markup Works

Markup measures how much is added on top of cost. If a product costs $50 and markup is 40%, the markup amount is $20 and the selling price is $70. That makes markup a direct pricing tool because it starts from the cost base you control.

Markup is not the same as margin. Markup is measured against cost. Margin is measured against selling price. The profit dollars are the same, but the percentage language is different. That distinction matters when finance teams talk in margin but pricing teams build from cost.

Formula:

- Selling price = Cost x (1 + Markup / 100)
- Markup percentage = ((Selling price - Cost) / Cost) x 100

## Single Product vs Basket View

Single-product mode is for one item, one job, or one quote. Basket mode is for a mixed set of products where the weighted result matters more than the average row.

That weighting matters. A low-cost accessory with a high markup does not influence the basket as much as a high-cost product with a lower markup. For that reason, basket markup is not a simple average of row percentages. It is a cost-weighted result across the whole group.

Use landed cost wherever possible. Freight, packaging, processing fees, and negotiated discounts can materially change the true markup, especially on lower-priced items.

## Example Scenario

Assume a store buys an item for $32 and wants a 50% markup.

- Markup amount = $32 x 0.50 = $16
- Selling price = $32 + $16 = $48

Now assume that item sits inside a basket with other products. If the basket contains one high-cost product and several cheap accessories, the basket markup will be driven mainly by the high-cost line, not by the cheapest rows. That is why basket review is useful for real catalog pricing.

## Common Mistakes

- Confusing markup with margin
- Using incomplete cost inputs
- Averaging product markups instead of weighting the basket
- Ignoring discounting or channel fees
- Using list price instead of actual selling price in reverse calculations

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do you calculate price from cost and markup?

Multiply cost by one plus the markup rate as a decimal.

### How do you calculate markup from cost and price?

Subtract cost from price, divide by cost, then multiply by 100.

### Is markup the same as margin?

No. Markup is based on cost, while margin is based on selling price.

### Why is basket markup not a simple average?

Because the combined result is weighted by total cost and quantity, not by row count.

### Can markup be negative?

Yes. That means the selling price is below cost.

### What is the most common mistake?

Using incomplete cost inputs and overstating the true markup.
